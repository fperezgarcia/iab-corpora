						<blockquote class="postcontent restore ">
							as diesel is widely used in some overseas markets, some japanese manufacturers had developed some interesting concepts about 2-stroke diesel engines, lighter than its european 4-stroke competitors... unfortunately these good ideas were not introduced into production models...<br/>
<br/>
as 2-stroke engines are lighter, have a better power-to-weight ratio and are cheaper to produce, would have been a good alternative during the recent economic recession...<br/>
<br/>
<br/>
<img src="http://www.yamaha-motor.co.jp/global/news/1999/03/23/images/engine.jpg" border="0" alt=""/><br/>
<a href="http://www.yamaha-motor.co.jp/global/news/1999/03/23/super-diesel.html" target="_blank">http://www.yamaha-motor.co.jp/global...er-diesel.html</a><br/>
<br/>
yamaha sd (super diesel) 60 cu.in. without any supercharging... this 2cyl engine was proposed in 1999 and met euro4 standards (with the improvements on emissions control systems it could be tuned to meet euro5 standards easily, or even california standards) and had variable compression ratio...<br/>
<br/>
i believe it could be good in a chevrolet metro, or an aveo...<br/>
<br/>
<br/>
<br/>
<br/>
in the same year daihatsu had shown a 3cyl supercharged 60cu.in. <a href="http://www.daihatsu.com/brand/motorshow/tokyo99/2cd/" target="_blank">http://www.daihatsu.com/brand/motorshow/tokyo99/2cd/</a><br/>
this one seems to be enough even to a chevy cobalt or a hhr, but would need more r &amp; d to meet current emission standards...<br/>
<br/>
<br/>
then in 2003 daihatsu had shown a 660cc 2cyl supercharged AND turbocharged, topaz 2cddi engine... <a href="http://www.daihatsu.com/brand/motorshow/tokyo03/engine/index.html" target="_blank">http://www.daihatsu.com/brand/motors...ine/index.html</a><br/>
<br/>
<br/>
<br/>
currently, there are just few developments in this area, most of them by australian engineering company orbital engines <a href="http://www.orbeng.com.au/" target="_blank">http://www.orbeng.com.au/</a> <a href="http://www.flexdi.com/" target="_blank">http://www.flexdi.com/</a> but they are focusing more in spark-ignited engines to increase the cold start capability...
						</blockquote>

							Now that's interesting.  I love two strokes!  Nice quick power. The Detroit has the sexy sound.  Fastest diesel that I know of is on Utube. Detroit in a Ford 1/2 ton.<br/>
<br/>
I've often wondered if a gas two stroke could be made to burn diesel.  According to the Bosch Bible diesel is far easier to ignite than gas!<br/>
<br/>
That would be a rewarding enterprise. Take an old 2 stroke gasser and try to make it burn diesel. I'll bet you could find a ratio of gas to diesel where the engine would carburate and run.  Its easy to change jets in a Mikuni, Keinen or Bing.  Cut the head down to raise the compression.  Start it on gas, get it good and hot, switch over to diesel, cut out the spark.  Carburated diesel.  Now that's a thought.  Wonder if the sloppy timing would be a problem.  Oh well, figure out an injection system.

				<div class="content">
					<div id="post_message_202973">
						<blockquote class="postcontent restore ">
							One of the potential advantages of a 2-stroke diesel is the elimination of the valve train and valves by using piston ports.  However, in the V and inline cylinder arrangement, it is difficult to properly scavenge the exhaust relying only on ports.  The GM diesels solved the problem by adding conventional exhaust valves at the top of the cylinder, effectively negating the lack-of-valve train advantage.<br/>
<br/>
A way to achieve effective exhaust scavenging is to use two opposed pistons in one cylinder.  The space between the piston heads become the combustion chamber with one piston's ports being used for intake and the other piston's ports for exhaust.  This arrangement provides effective flow through exhaust scavenging without conventional valves.  The down side is that two crank shaft are required.<br/>
<br/>
This arrangement was successfully used in the German Jumo diesel aircraft engine during WWII.  Fairbanks Morse also had a bigger version of this arrangement that was used in submarines and locomotives.<br/>
<br/>
The Brits went one better.  By using three crankshafts, arranged in a triangle, they tripled the number of pistons over a two crankshaft arrangement.  This was the Napier Deltic engine use in marine and locomotive applications.  Here is a video of the workings:  <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?feature=fvwp&amp;NR=1&amp;v=D3bj47TAYiU" target="_blank">http://www.youtube.com/watch?feature...&amp;v=D3bj47TAYiU</a><br/>
<br/>
What was really cool was the sound of the engine.  You expect a locomotive diesel to have a deep rumble.  The Deltics had more shriek <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=NjW-rybCWn0&amp;feature=related" target="_blank">http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=NjW-r...eature=related</a>
