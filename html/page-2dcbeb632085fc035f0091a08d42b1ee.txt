        <h1 class="entry-title"><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/automotive-histories/automotive-history-the-short-and-odd-life-of-the-two-door-station-wagon/" rel="bookmark">Automotive History: The Short And Odd Life Of The Two Door Station Wagon</a></h1><div class="entry-info"><div class="entry-author">By <address class="author vcard"><a class="url fn n" href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/author/paul/" title="Paul Niedermeyer">Paul Niedermeyer</a></address></div> &ndash; <abbr class="published" title="2011-10-19T04:17:21+00:00">Posted on October 19, 2011</abbr></div>        
        <div class="entry-content clearfix">
		<p><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Plymout-1960-2-door-wagon.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16324" title="1960 Plymouth Station Wagon" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Plymout-1960-2-door-wagon.jpg" alt="" width="500" height="261" /></a></p>
<p>Who&#8217;s idea was that? Along with the business coupe and the Ranchero/El Camino utility pickups, large two door station wagon ranks right up there in the Pantheon of American car oddities. One can even make a pretty good argument that it towers above them, given its questionable heritage and utility. The business coupe&#8217;s origins go back to the earliest coupes, <a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/automotive-histories/automotive-history-trying-to-make-business-coupe-sense-of-the-gremlin/">like the Model T</a>, when roadsters and two-passenger coupes were were common. And the utilities were just an update on those same coupes and roadsters with little beds grafted onto them, the pickup&#8217;s precursor. But for about a dozen years or so, Detroit graced Americans with something they had no idea they needed: the two door wagon. <span id="more-16321"></span></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Buick-woodie-wagon.png"><img class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-16325" title="Buick woodie wagon" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Buick-woodie-wagon-1024x567.png" alt="" width="558" height="309" /></a></p>
<p>Before WW2, the station wagon was primarily a commercially-oriented  vehicle, and given its intended role in hauling large numbers  of people and their cargo, always with four doors (or more, like this handsome <span style="text-decoration: line-through;">Buick</span> LaSalle) . A two-door wagon? It&#8217;s possible that someone will prove me wrong and show me that a production two door wagon existed before WW2. Station wagons then were almost always built by outside contractors, using plenty of wood, and at least four doors. And almost all of them were registered as commercial vehicles, given their commercial chassis underpinnings and typical role. But like so many things automotive and otherwise, the post war era unleashed big changes.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/willys-1946.png"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16326" title="willys 1946" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/willys-1946.png" alt="" width="578" height="406" /></a></p>
<p>Ironically, it all started with the maker of that WW2 icon, the Jeep. Desperate for a new market, Willys brought to out their quite revolutionary all-steel wagon (the wood grain is painted on). Given that this was not intended to directly compete directly with the existing station wagon market, and rode on a substantially shorter wheelbase, two doors certainly made sense.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Willys-1949-wagon.png"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16327" title="Willys 1949 wagon" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Willys-1949-wagon.png" alt="" width="572" height="320" /></a></p>
<p>The Willys was also significantly shorter, and not available as a three-row wagon. Rightfully, it could be called a compact, and was available with a four or six. In a world of strictly genuine-wood four-door wagons, the Willys was a true revolutionary.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/CCC-Plymouth-wagon2.png"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16328" title="Plymouth wagon" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/CCC-Plymouth-wagon2.png" alt="" width="574" height="366" /></a></p>
<p>Plymouth clearly was heavily influenced by the Willys wagon, and introduced their own all-steel two-door wagon in 1949. This marked the first all-steel wagon by the Big Three, and was made right on the same assembly lines as the sedans. The Plymouth two-door wagon sat on the smaller 111&#8243; wheelbase of the junior P 17 Plymouths, which only came in two doors variants anyway. The larger P 18 series had a traditional woody four door wagon, so both bases were covered. The Plymouth wagon was a big hit, and given its rugged construction, could still be seen in its role of hauling families well into the sixties.</p>
<p><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Ford-1949_Ford_Country_Squire_Woody_Survivor_For_Sale_Front_1.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16323" title="Ford 1949 Country Squire" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Ford-1949_Ford_Country_Squire_Woody_Survivor_For_Sale_Front_1.jpg" alt="" width="479" height="340" /></a></p>
<p>Ford really jumped into the two-door wagon pool with both feet in 1949. Unlike Plymouth, Ford only had two-door wagons for their 1949 &#8211; 1951 models, including the nine-passenger Country Squire. In a bit of an odd twist, Ford kept the traditional wood construction. Might as well be sure you&#8217;re going to float after taking that plunge.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Ford-country-squire-interior-1951.png"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16330" title="Ford country squire interior 1951" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Ford-country-squire-interior-1951.png" alt="" width="506" height="381" /></a></p>
<p>The logic of building a two-door three-row wagon, with the third row still facing forward, would be mighty difficult to muster in today&#8217;s obesity-challenged world. Folks still knew how to bend and clamber.</p>
<p><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Ford-49-wagon-ad.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16329" title="Ford 49 wagon ad" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Ford-49-wagon-ad.jpg" alt="" width="368" height="500" /></a></p>
<p>And there they are, all happily ensconced, including adults in the third row. Well, the Ford certainly was a nice looking wagon, until the lacquer peeled off the wood and it started to fade and rot.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Chevrolet-1949-wagon.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16331" title="Chevrolet 1949 wagon" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Chevrolet-1949-wagon.jpg" alt="" width="512" height="353" /></a></p>
<p>Chevrolet went the other way, offering only four door wagons. These tall wagons were quite roomy too, although maybe not quite so as is depicted in the ever-unfaithful renderings of the time. Makes it look like a bus in there.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Chevrolet-1955-2door-wagons.png"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16333" title="Chevrolet 1955  2door wagons" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Chevrolet-1955-2door-wagons.png" alt="" width="524" height="289" /></a></p>
<p>Ford added four door wagons by 1952, and kept both body styles in production. But Chevrolet held out until 1955, when the Handyman low-level two door wagons were introduced,</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/CC-145-090-800.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16334" title="nomad" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/CC-145-090-800.jpg" alt="" width="560" height="404" /></a></p>
<p>as well as the very upscale Nomad (<a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/blog/cc-capsule-1955-chevrolet-nomad-stealing-the-thunder-from-the-high-priced-cars/">CC here</a>), which had its own unique sheet metal. Suddenly, the questionable identity of the two-door wagon became really fragmented, populating the very low and high end of the station wagon pecking order. With the Nomad, the idea of the wagon as a sporty, upscale vehicle took hold, but its life as a two-door was quite short, ending after the 1957 model. But its influence would be felt to some degree or another throughout the rest of the station wagon&#8217;s tenure.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Ford-1956-Parklane.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-16341" title="Ford 1956 Parklane" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Ford-1956-Parklane-1024x779.jpg" alt="" width="558" height="424" /></a></p>
<p>Ford responded to the Nomad with their 1956 Parklane, but it was really just a gussied up two-door Ranch wagon.</p>
<p><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Plymouth-1955-2-door-wagon.png"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16338" title="Plymouth 1955 2 door wagon" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Plymouth-1955-2-door-wagon.png" alt="" width="585" height="307" /></a></p>
<p>Plymouth didn&#8217;t bother, and the two-door wagons were just the typical bottom of the barrel variety.</p>
<p><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Plymouth-2-door-wagon.png"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16342" title="Plymouth 2 door wagon" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Plymouth-2-door-wagon.png" alt="" width="530" height="261" /></a></p>
<p>Although with the all-new 1957 models, the low end Plymouth wagons can hardly be called lack-luster. In fact, they&#8217;re quite dramatic. But I can&#8217;t recall seeing one in the flesh, rotted or just just spongy. Big Plymouth and Dodge wagons of the time were popular with big families, but they mostly stuck to the four door versions.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Mercury-1957-Commuter-Wagon.png"><img class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-16424" title="Mercury 1957 Commuter Wagon" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Mercury-1957-Commuter-Wagon-1024x556.png" alt="" width="558" height="302" /></a></p>
<p>Mercury was notoriously flamboyant in the 1957 &#8211; 1959 era, and they fielded not just a two-door wagon, but a two-door hardtop wagon! Now we&#8217;re really starting to make sense! Unless I&#8217;ve really blown it, Mercury was the only one to do so. Well somebody just had to.</p>
<p><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Edsel-1958-Roundup-wagon.png"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16425" title="Edsel 1958 Roundup wagon" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Edsel-1958-Roundup-wagon.png" alt="" width="421" height="214" /></a></p>
<p>That made me wonder if Edsel got to share that body shell too. No such luck; instead they had to make do with the lowly Ford Ranch Wagon body, and a name that would really resonate today: Edsel Roundup, guaranteed to kill something.</p>
<p><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Chevrolet-1960-2-door-wagon.png"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16344" title="Chevrolet 1960 2 door wagon" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Chevrolet-1960-2-door-wagon.png" alt="" width="507" height="215" /></a></p>
<p>The big-bodied Big Three cars of this period rightfully marked the end of the whole foolish exercise. It&#8217;s not like most folks couldn&#8217;t afford the extra eighty bucks to get you that second set of doors. But with a wave of new compacts about to make the scene, giant finned two-door wagons suddenly just seemed ridiculous. 1960 marked the last year for the big Chevy Brookwood two-door.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Ford-1961-Ranch-wagon-2-door.png"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16345" title="Ford 1961 Ranch wagon 2 door" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Ford-1961-Ranch-wagon-2-door.png" alt="" width="540" height="270" /></a></p>
<p>Ford lasted one year longer, with this quite rare 1961 two-door Ranch Wagon.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Plymouth-1961-2-door-wagon.png"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16346" title="Plymouth 1961 2 door wagon" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Plymouth-1961-2-door-wagon.png" alt="" width="596" height="271" /></a></p>
<p>And Plymouth also bowed out after 1961. Any of these later year two-door wagons are hot commodities now. But what about the independents. Where they true to their name, and stayed out of the two-door wagon mania?</p>
<p><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Rambler-1950-1952-rambler-4.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16347" title="Rambler 1950-1952-" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Rambler-1950-1952-rambler-4.jpg" alt="" width="400" height="204" /></a></p>
<p>One can excuse the compact little Rambler, especially since all the bigger Rambler wagons were exclusively four doors, and it was so damn cute anyway.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Studebaker-1954-wagon.png"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16348" title="Studebaker 1954 wagon" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Studebaker-1954-wagon.png" alt="" width="508" height="227" /></a></p>
<p>Studebaker didn&#8217;t even enter the wagon market until 1954, and then only with a two-door. But a four door came along a couple of years later.</p>
<p><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/studebaker_champion_scotsman_wagon-bw.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16349" title="studebaker_champion_scotsman_wagon-bw" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/studebaker_champion_scotsman_wagon-bw.jpg" alt="" width="480" height="350" /></a></p>
<p>And Studebaker took the whole stripper concept to its ultimate and most starkly naked extreme. In 1957, the Scotsman sedan and wagon were devoid of every possible amenity, including chrome-plated hub caps. Even wasting color film on publicity shots would have been all wrong with this el-cheapo special. And just try using that name today.</p>
<p><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Studebaker-Lark-1959_Ad-03.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16354" title="Studebaker Lark 1959_Ad-03" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Studebaker-Lark-1959_Ad-03.jpg" alt="" width="398" height="500" /></a></p>
<p>The 1959 Lark may have been promoted as a thrifty compact, but it had the same basic Studebaker body, minus the goofy front end. And the whole Scotsman austerity theme was out, as this ad makes quite clear. Small doesn&#8217;t have to be spartan. Especially when small is in.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Ford-1960-falcon-tudor-wagon.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16426" title="Ford 1960-falcon-tudor-wagon" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Ford-1960-falcon-tudor-wagon.jpg" alt="" width="567" height="223" /></a></p>
<p>The new 1960 compacts took different approaches to their wagons. Falcon (and Comet), fielded both versions, to great success. The Valiant stuck to four doors only.</p>
<p><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/PlymouthValiantWagon.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16427" title="PlymouthValiantWagon" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/PlymouthValiantWagon.jpg" alt="" width="498" height="240" /></a></p>
<p>I shouldn&#8217;t really show it here, but it was such an ugly duck. No wonder the Falcon wagon totally creamed it. And good luck try finding one of the early Valiant wagons.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/CC-107-065-800.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16428" title="corvair" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/CC-107-065-800.jpg" alt="" width="560" height="326" /></a></p>
<p>And the Corvair arrived in 1961 with four door wagons only too. Given that the Chevy II was due the following year, having two compact four door wagons made no sense. Chevy should have made a Nomad-ish Corvair wagon, as a companion to the sporty Monza coupe. Unlike the total dud Lakewood wagon, that could well have been a hit.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Ford-falcon-1965-tudor-wagon.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16430" title="1965 falcon tudor wagon" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Ford-falcon-1965-tudor-wagon.jpg" alt="" width="540" height="405" /></a></p>
<p>So now we get to the end of the story. What was the last two-door wagon standing? Well two actually. The Falcon&#8217;s last year for that body style was 1965, which was really just an updated 1960. How&#8217;d you like to have that one, with a 289 and stick shift?</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Chevelle-64-wagon.png"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16431" title="Chevelle 64 wagon" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Chevelle-64-wagon.png" alt="" width="574" height="338" /></a></p>
<p>But it was Chevy that revived the two door again, as a fresh new body style of the all-new 1964 Chevelle. It had a very obvious Nomad-inspired side window treatment and B-pillar, but was available only as a low-trim Chevelle 300.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Chevelle-1964-2-door-wagon1.png"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16432" title="Chevelle 1964 2 door wagon" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Chevelle-1964-2-door-wagon1.png" alt="" width="571" height="346" /></a></p>
<p>Why Chevrolet bother to tool this up, when none of the other competing intermediates were offering a two-door wagon is hard to fathom in the first place. But having done so, why they didn&#8217;t offer it as a sporty Nomad is even harder comprehend. Can you imagine what a cult status a &#8217;65 Malibu Nomad SS 396 would have?</p>
<p><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Chevelle-1965-wagon.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16433" title="Chevelle 1965 wagon" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Chevelle-1965-wagon.jpg" alt="" width="500" height="334" /></a></p>
<p>Needless to say, these &#8217;64 &#8211; &#8217;65 Chevelle 300 two-door wagons have pretty much all been appropriated by the go-fast crowd, for obvious reasons. And who can blame them, since Chevy missed out on such an opportunity to revive the Nomad.</p>
<p><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Pontiac-gto-64-crop1.png"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16435" title="Pontiac gto 64 " src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Pontiac-gto-64-crop1.png" alt="" width="366" height="272" /></a></p>
<p>One last speculation: why didn&#8217;t John DeLorean appropriate the Chevelle two-door wagon body for Pontiac, and turn it into a GTO shooting brake? Sounds like it just needs to be done; at least on photoshop. Any volunteers?</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Pontiac-1955-Safari-1.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16436" title="Pontiac 1955 Safari" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Pontiac-1955-Safari-1.jpg" alt="" width="512" height="379" /></a></p>
<p>There would be precedence, of course, as Pontiac got their sticky fingers on the original Nomad.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Buick-vista-wagon-2-door.png"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16438" title="Buick sportwagon 2 door" src="http://www.curbsideclassic.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Buick-vista-wagon-2-door.png" alt="" width="556" height="203" /></a></p>
<p>Someone&#8217;s gone and done it the 1964 Buick Sportwagon, and that&#8217;s not a photoshop. Maybe we&#8217;ll leave it at that, since this Sportwagon makes as much or more sense then most of the production two-door wagons. Let&#8217;s face it, the lasting impact of the two door wagon was hardly its utility, but as a plaything and oddity. And if you&#8217;re going to play, might as well make a sport of it.</p>

<p>Some commercial radio outfit in Tacoma had a fleet of 1957 and 1958 Plymouth 2-door wagons to run parts, and some of them ended up at my neighborhood wrecking yard after their tour of duty was completed. These were six-cylinder, three-speed cars with only a heater, and on top of that were light gray. They were long-wheelbase though and could probably haul lots of stuff if it wasn&#8217;t too heavy. The 57-59 Mopar wagons were all built on a long wheelbase &#8211; I suspect so that the stampings could be used for the Chrysler wagons &#8211; and had their own fin designs and taillights. This oddity wasn&#8217;t restricted to the 2-doors though.</p>

A two-door wagon would be a cheaply-produced hybrid of a sedan-delivery and a family car.</p>
<p>And it would make perfect sense, if one had a brood of young&#8217;uns and was worried about one of them playing with door latches.  In those pre-computer days, devices that locked passenger doors from the driver&#8217;s seat were limited to a few luxury brands.  And &#8220;childproof locks&#8221; were, to most children, a challenge&#8230;for a few minutes (I know; been there, done that!)</p>
<p>I&#8217;ve always thought the two-door wagon to be the second-most versatile carbody style&#8230;the first being a full-size van.  Cargo space is enclosed and out of the weather; but it&#8217;s THERE.  The body, without all those doors, has more structural integrity.  And, from my emotional reaction, it makes a wagon less of a &#8220;family car&#8221; &#8211; for a young buck with toys but no family.</p>

One great feature was the little &#8220;hallway&#8221; than ran alongside the middle seat to get to the back. It was slightly inclined, and my smallest brother and smaller sister took great pleasure in amusing themselves by marching up and down it endlessly, yo-yo-ing between the middle and back seats during longish trips.<br />
The third seat was the &#8220;premium&#8221; position for us kids to fight over (it was farthest away from the adults&#8230;). Being the largest and quickest of the siblings, I was usually able to scurry there first to claim my &#8220;command post&#8221; for most voyages.<br />
Since the 3rd seat was behind the back seat position of mundane passenger cars, we kids referred to it as the &#8220;backer-acker&#8221; seat.<br />
That wagon earned its keep as a work horse vehicle, carrying all six of us, complete with tents, sleeping bags and cooking gear (a major portion of it on a roof rack) on several memorable camping trips in the Northeast States.<br />
Its replacement was a &#8217;55 Ford Ranchwagon, another 2-door, which continued the tradition by carrying us on camping trips that ultimately took us from coast to coast. We kids were not so thrilled by lack of a 3rd seat &#8211; no hallway to negotiate and one of us had to lie on TOP of all the camping gear that was packed into the rear space in order to streamline down the size of the roof mounted gear.</p>
