                                    <h1 class="title">Driving the Saab 9-3 Convertible: A <i>FUN</i> Car That Actually Makes Sense...</h1>
                                    <span class="rgr"><br>by mrkstvns:&nbsp;Feb 21, 2007</span><br><br>
                                    

                                    <span class="sgr"><img src="http://img.epinions.com/images/epi_images/e3/4_big_stars.gif" alt="Product Rating: 4.0" width="83" height="15" hspace="0" vspace="0" border="0"></span>
                                    <span class="rkb" style="margin: 0 0 0 25px; vertical-align: top;">Recommended:&nbsp;Yes&nbsp;<img src="http://img.epinions.com/images/epi_images/cars/thumb_up.gif" style="vertical-align: top;"/></span>
                                    
                                    <br><br>

                                    
                                    <span class="rkr">
                                    
                                    
                                    <b>Pros:</b>&nbsp;Safety first, even with top down fun!<br>
                                    <b>Cons:</b>&nbsp;Quirky<br>
                                    
                                    
                                    <b>The Bottom Line:</b>&nbsp;Style, substance, and a whole lot of driving fun. That's what the Saab 9-3 is all about. Read on to see why...<br><br>
                                    
                                    
                                    
                                </td>
                            </tr>
                            <tr>
                                <td style="padding: 0 20 0 20; border-left: #BBD6EF solid 1px;"><hr style="height:0; border:0; border-top: 1px solid #BBD6EF;"></td>
                            </tr>
                            <tr>
                                <td style="border-right: 0px; padding: 20px; border-left: #BBD6EF solid 1px;">
                                    <div class="rkr">
                                    
                                        
                                        
                                        I still remember one of the first car jokes I ever heard as a kid: What's the most dangerous part of a car? The nut behind the wheel. <br>
<br>
There's probably not a whole lot that a carmaker can do to fix <i>that</i> problem, but if a way ever does get invented, it will probably be one of those zany Swedish car engineers who cracks the tough nut. Everybody thinks Volvo when it comes to safety, but Saab is no slouch for safety engineering themselves. Fortunately, both Volvo and Saab also make some very comfortable, inviting, and dare I say it....<i>sporty</i> looking vehicles. I'd definitely count the Saab 9-3 in that category.<br>
<br>
I recently got the chance to drive a Saab 9-3 convertible, and although it doesn't have the goods to wow me straight across the board, it does do a great job in a lot of important areas, and I could easily see this car sitting in my garage if the numbers spun the right way. Let's take a look...<br>
<br>
<br>
<b>An Approving Nod: <i>Exterior Styling & Curb Appeal</i>...</b><br>
Ever since GM took over the reigns of Saab, I've been worried that the oddly quirky and aerodynamic "inspired by jets" design philosophy of Saab would get replaced by the "inspired by a butt-ugly Chevy Cavalier" philosophy. <br>
<br>
That did indeed happen with the 9-7X --- a vehicle I wouldn't buy in my most horrific nightmare --- but the 9-3 and the 9-5 lines continue to look, well, like Saabs.<br>
<br>
I like the way the Saab 9-3 looks. From the front, it looks bold and stylish. From the profile view, it looks like a sleek wedge, ready to slice through the air with all the aerodynamic authority of a stealth fighter. While it shares much in common with all the sleek vehicles of this class, it's also got quirks in the design that give it an unmistakable Saab flavor. Like the slick little side vent inlets in the front grill, or the high-set hips of its blocky back-end. <br>
<br>
It's a good looking ride, even if the lines are somewhat muted and understated. It's not pretentious or flashy, just competently sleek because that's the shape that makes sense.<br>
<br>
<br>
<b>Oddly Comfortable: <i>Interior Factors</i>...</b><br>
Inside the 9-3 is another matter. I can't decide if I absolutely love its gentle eccentricities and basic comfort, or if I want to scream at the persistent ergonomics of yesterdecade.  Perhaps its that "born from jets mentality" again, that makes Saab want to have the dashboards look like airplane cockpits. If you're the kind of person who just <i>loves</i> pushing buttons, then drive a Saab. <br>
<br>
I realize that the dashboard is one of those "quirks" that makes the car a Saab and not just another standard interchangeable cog in the auto industry machine, but at some point you're bound to go bonkers over the <i>dozens</i> of doo-dad controls and buttons all over that center panel of climate controls and stereo controls. <br>
<br>
Fundamental functions are hard to figure out with the Saab, and don't make much sense even if you do figure it out. Some things are just pure gimmick --- like the little joystick controllers on the air vents. Some are pure excess.  <br>
<br>
Your basic speed, tach, and fuel and temp gauges are all clustered where they make sense, and they're all plainly visible in normal driving conditions. The dash does indeed sit up higher than it does on most cars, and feels like it's more "in your face". But that contributes to it's sense of Saab. So too does the odd key fob and the odd keyhole nestled in the bottom of the center console. <br>
<br>
But if it's "weirdness" you're after, stick a hand in the glove box. Is it freakin' <i>cold</i> in there or what? Believe it or not, the Saab 9-3 does indeed have a chilled glove box. Of what <i>use</i> this is, I'm not sure, but it's a good bet that one evening Officer Hardass is gonna pull me over and I'll be heard saying, "Why, yes I do have proof of insurance. It's right here under these chilly brewskis, kept at a perfect 40 degrees in my handy-dandy chilled glove box...." <br>
<br>
In the 9-3 convertible, the supple leather front seats are a dream to sit in. Plenty of lateral support, firm, contoured rear support, plus 8-way electronic seat controls. Unfortunately, there's no memory on them (though I understand that a 3-setting memory function is available as an option). Electronic seat controls are likewise only on the drivers seat (passenger seat controls are standard only in the 9-3's Aero configuration). There's more than ample leg room up front, which is more than can be said of the back seat.<br>
<br>
Although there is a real back seat in a Saab 9-3 convertible, it's not at all as spacious as the generous 4-door sedan back seat. In the convertible, the rear seat spans only about 2/3 the width of the car, and its moved further forward to accomodate the convertible top and an expanded trunk. Translation: Any human with legs will not want to sit in the back seat. Any human who attempts to do so will <i>become</i> a human without legs, and can henceforward sit in the rear seat in spacious, legless comfort.<br>
<br>
Don't laugh it up. That's not a joke, son. The front seats <i>do</i> push back to within less than 1 inch of the rear seat. It's a cool convertible, but if you pack 4 passengers into it, they're gonna be sardines (or legless --- your choice).<br>
<br>
Fun is really the reason to buy the 9-3 convertible. The 9-3 is a nice ride to begin with, but with the drop-top, it becomes a serious joy. And while most wives won't let their husbands get 2-seat roadsters, they might be persuaded into a "family oriented car" that can drop its top. (Well, you can try anyway). And you're just 20 seconds from top-up to top-down, and it can be dropped with a mere push of a button on the keyfob. <br>
<br>
<br>
<b>Wahooooo!! <i>Performance Factors</i>...</b><br>
Charles Dickens drove this car, and he said, "It was the best of cars, it was the worst of cars..."<br>
<br>
I know what the Chuckster means.<br>
<br>
A part of me wants to admit that front-wheel drive is A-okay, the 9-3 handles much better than your average mid-size sedan, and having two hundred horses is <i>more</i> than ample for any sane driver. <br>
<br>
Yet, a part of me hesitates, knowing that rear-wheel drive generally gives better performance --- at least in normal conditions (I'd still pick a front-wheel drive car on a snowy day). And a part of me wants to prefer the 300-hp powerplant that some of the mid-$30K sedans deliver these days --- whether its ridiculously excessive for the car's size and weight or not. <br>
<br>
Yet, driving the 9-3 equipped with the basic 2.0T powertrain says to me, "Unless you are a <i>serious</i> performance car driver, the Saab 9-3 is quick, agile, brakes like a champ, and is a total hoot to drive. The one option I'd need to have if I bought this car would be some sunblock to smear on my head, because I'd be cruising with the top down every freakin' day.<br>
<br>
Even though the spec sheet says 210 hp for the turbo-4 cylinder, stomp the gas as you head up the interstate entrance ramp and the car <i>jumps</i> out in front of the pack and zips you up to cruising speed effortlessly. It's no Lamborghini Murcielago, but it'll blow the doors off the Camrys and mini-vans, and that's all you probably want to ask of your car anyway. 210 horse, yeah, but it is indeed enough.<br>
<br>
As for agility and handling, it's pretty decent, and again, it will be more than adequate for any normal person. Yes, I am <i>certain</i> that a BMW 328 would out-corner it and could spin a tighter circle than the Saab 9-3....but could a Volvo C70? Probably not. The car feels tight on the turns and I like the competent feel of the steering (little knobs on the steering wheel and all...)<br>
<br>
This is a solid-feeling, solid-driving vehicle.<br>
<br>
<br>
<b>Survivor! <i>The Safety Factors</i>...</b><br>
I might have to rethink my usual bias in favor of Volvo. I've always loved Volvo's cars, but it's their long-standing reputation for unstinting safety engineering that's really endeared them to me. What I usually overlook is that Saab has that same kind of dedication, and that they might even be out-Volvoing the competition in terms of safety.<br>
<br>
The 9-3 is loaded with safety features that speak volumes about their priorities, and it's a hefty behemoth of a car to boot (with a 3,800 pound curb weight, you've got almost 2 tons of iron to back you up). Everybody's got ABS braking systems these days, but the Saab 9-3 takes it a step further with an added layer of automated control called "electronic brake-force distribution." They also include electronic stability controls, and traction control --- on top of a car that's already got extraordinary aerodynamics and an impossibly low center of gravity. Even if all the engineered-in safety features don't avoid an accident for you, there's airbags in every conceivable location, plus an active head restraint system. Slide an Elvis disc into the CD changer and start boppin' your head around to the killer beats, and that system reaches out and restrains your head, admonishing you in a sexy Swedish accent to "Stop that this instant! That is unsafe behavior!" Okay, okay, so that's not really how it works....<br>
<br>
I'm a big fan of facts and reality, and while opinions are all well and good, and washlists of features are easy to use, they don't cut through the bull. To get past the smoke and mirrors, check out what some <i>experts</i> the car safety field have to say. Go check out the info on the NHTSA and the IIHS web sites. If you do, be prepared for the Saab to <i>astound</i> you...<br>
<br>
The Saab 9-3 has veritably perfect safety scores, right across the board. If you put your stock in those NHTSA 5-star ratings, well then, Saab is in your sweet spot. If you put your faith in the dollars-and-sense bottom line of the guys over at IIHS, then you'll probably be more impressed that the 2007 Saab 9-3 is their "top safety pick" among mid-size cars.<br>
<br>
If safety is your number 1 criterion in buying a new car, you should just log off this computer right now and hightail it over to your Saab dealer. The 9-3 is <i>your</i> car. Crumple zone, schmumple zone, Saab builds in the safety stuff that other car makers won't have for years. <br>
<br>
'nuff said on all that.<br>
<br>
<br>
<b>All In the Family: <i>The 9-3 Model Line</i>...</b><br>
I'm driving the 9-3 convertible in its 2.0T configuration with the 5-speed automatic transmission. It's a fun car to drive. It's got some attitude. But it won't be for everyone. Maybe one of the <i>other</i> 9-3 models will be a better fit for your needs...<br>
<br>
The 9-3 is available in 3 body styles (4-door sedan, 2-door convertible, 4-door hatchback wagon) each of which is available in one of two powertrain configurations: 2.0T or Aero. What's the difference? Ooohhh...about 5 grand. <br>
<br>
2.0T is the entry-level powertrain. It gives you a turbocharged 4-cylinder engine, 6-speed manual transmission, 16 inch wheels, and a pretty darn nicely configured interior space. Pony up the extra bucks and the Aero gives you 2 extra cylinders under the hood, cranking out another 40 horses in the power department, plus another 40 foot pounds or so of torque. It gives you another inch under the wheel wells too, plus a sport-tuned suspension that translates to better agility in the turns. <br>
<br>
While I see the important differences between the 2.0T and Aero lines being in the performance department, the Aero does also bring a few interior niceties to the table as well. Like a power passenger seat (only the driver gets seat-mounted toys on the 2.0T), or an upgraded stereo that puts out 300 watts instead of 150, as does the stereo on the 2.0T models. <br>
<br>
So what'll it be? Convertible? Sedan? Combi (as the wagon body is known in Saab-speak)? Turbo 4, or Turbo 6? 6-speed manual or automatic? <br>
<br>
Personally, I think the 2.0T sedan is the best bang-for-the-buck of all the 9-3 models, but of course, your priorities might spin differently and your mileage may vary. See your friendly neighborhood Saab dealer for details. Yada yada yada...<br>
<br>
<br>
<b>Photo Finish: <i>Saab 9-3's Competitive Position</i>...</b><br>
The Saab 9-3 is a sophisticated car that appeals to a non-mainstream buyer. It's got more refinements than any of your mainstream mass market brands, but perhaps less well executed than you'd find in your true top-line luxury brands. But for the price, it's an extremely strong competitor. <br>
<br>
I'd compare this vehicle directly to any European-designed convertible coupe, namely the convertible styles of the Audi A4, BMW 328, Volvo C70, and Mercedes-Benz CLK350. You could even throw in the Volkswagen Eos, Toyota Solara, and Pontiac G6 convertibles, just to liven up the competition a bit...<br>
<br>
In my opinion, the Saab sedan could possibly beat much of this pack with its combination of comfort and function in the 4-door interior, quirky styling and curb appeal, outstanding safety engineering, and the practical backing of a GM 100,000 mile powertrain warranty. That would tip me to Saab's favor over the Audi A4 and the Volvo C70. The little Benz is a nice looking rag top, but I think it's priced beyond what it gives you for the money (the BMW 328 is a <i>far</i> better executed vehicle at a better price point).<br>
<br>
I always love the flawless design execution and near perfect response and agility that BMW embodies, and head-to-head against a 328, I'd probably go for the Bemer (unless my wife sees this review, in which case she'll veto me and pick the Saab for it's flawless safety execution --- we all have different priorities). <br>
<br>
Of course, numbers can also sway me at any given time, and Saab has been lately offering $1,500 cash back on the '07 models...and that 100K warranty <i>does</i> appeal to me...especially since both Consumer Reports and JD Powers have noted that owners have reported some trouble spots on the Saab 9-3's engine. But as always, your mileage may vary... <br>
<br>
<br>
<b>Bottom Line...</b><br>
I like this Saab 9-3....quite a lot. I don't really want Saab to change anything that would undermine the unique "Saabness" of this car, but I do think I'd go bonkers with daily exposure to such poor ergonomic design as this car displays. Despite that, it's a relatively comfortable ride, a fairly solid performer, and a real value when you think about how much more "coolness" you get with a Saab 9-3 for just a little more cash than the price of much less interesting and far more pedestrian vehicles from Japanese or domestic automakers. A lot of what you want in a high-end brand is in the Saab, but at a better deal. The safety is second to none, the warranty is best in the business, and with comfort, style, and a decent price, what's not to like??<br>
<br>
Give it a drive!<br>
