		<h1 class="entry-header">Hyundai Elantra Electric Concept at the 2012 Detroit Auto Show</h1>
	
	<div class="entry-content">
		<div class="entry-body">
			<p><img alt="HyundaiElantraElectric" border="0" class="asset  asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451b3c669e20167604d00f0970b" src="http://blogs.cars.com/.a/6a00d83451b3c669e20167604d00f0970b-800wi" title="HyundaiElantraElectric" /></p>
<ul>
<li> <strong>Looks like: </strong>The gas-only Elantra, with a big helping of EV stickers</li>
<li> <strong>Defining characteristics: </strong>All-electric drivetrain, 124-mile range, 27-kilowatt-hour lithium-polymer battery pack, 90 mph top speed</li>
<li> <strong>Ridiculous features:</strong> In the past, maybe the electric drivetrain, but no more</li>
<li> <strong>Chance of being mass-produced:</strong> Hyundai says this is just a concept for now</li>
</ul>
<p>Not to be left out of the electric-vehicle frenzy, Hyundai showed an all-electric version of its Elantra compact sedan at the 2012 Detroit auto show. It&#39;s purely a concept car at the moment, but it&#39;s yet more evidence of the weight automakers are giving to electric cars.</p>
<p><strong><a href="http://www.cars.com/autoshows/detroit/" target="_self">More 2012 Detroit Auto Show Coverage</a></strong></p>
<p>If it weren&#39;t for the elaborate graphics the Elantra Electric wouldn&#39;t have trouble blending in with its gas-only counterpart. The concept also gets a few extra badges, slightly different wheels and a unique Hyundai badge on the grille.</p>

		</div>
		
			<a id="more"></a>
				

<p>The big changes, however, are beneath the sheet metal, where the conventional four-cylinder powertrain has been swapped with an electric drivetrain. It consists of an 80-kilowatt motor powered by a 27-kilowatt-hour lithium-polymer battery pack. Hyundai claims the combination provides a driving range of 124 miles. The 6.6-kilowatt onboard charger enables a depleted battery to be replenished in five hours on 220-volt service, according to Hyundai.</p>
