    <h2><span style="font-size: 150%;">Top 5 Convertible Car Seats</span></h2>
<p><a href="#SUMMARY">[just tell me what to buy]</a></p>
<p>When your baby outgrows her infant seat, most parents then purchase a convertible car seat.&nbsp;</p>
<p><span class="full-image-float-left ssNonEditable"><img src="/storage/carseats/baby.png?__SQUARESPACE_CACHEVERSION=1355290044406" alt="" /></span></p>
<p><span style="font-size: 110%;"><strong>In a Nutshell</strong></span></p>
<p>A convertible seat is called so because it starts out rear-facing, usually for babies from 12-24&nbsp; months (or more), then converts to a forward-facing seat for 2+ year olds.</p>
<p>There are also &ldquo;all-in-one&rdquo; seats, such as the Evenflo Symphony.&nbsp;These seats can be used rear-facing, forward-facing, or as a BPB (belt-positioning booster seat). &ldquo;Well hell, why doesn&rsquo;t everyone just buy an all-in-one seat?&rdquo; you ask?&nbsp;Well, because most of them are not tall enough for bigger kids who still need to be in a booster; <strong>translation</strong>: you would still have to purchase another/taller booster seat anyway.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Some parents also opt for a convertible seat straight from birth to prevent having to buy two seats. These people need to pay special attention to lowest harness strap height (see below, &ldquo;Special Situations, #3&rdquo;)</p>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><strong>Size and Weight&nbsp;</strong></span></p>
<p>Compared to infant seats, convertible seats are monstrously big &mdash; they look like giant baby thrones. With a few exceptions, they are also quite heavy (20+ lbs). Unlike with infant seats, weight is not as big of a deal because you&rsquo;re not going to be taking it in and out of your car every day and plopping it into a stroller.</p>
<p>The recent trend is to keep children in a <em>harnessed</em> seat (as opposed to a booster seat) for as long as possible &ndash; simply because it&rsquo;s safer. You see, ten years ago, most convertible seats were rated only up to 40lbs or so; in the last few years they&rsquo;ve become much taller and rated for higher weight so that young kids can stay harnessed longer. <em>This is a good thing because the vast majority of parents have been putting their kids in booster seats far too early. </em></p>
<p>Nowadays, you&rsquo;ll see many seats going up to 70 or even 80lbs. Just remember, though, that <strong>a child will almost always outgrow a seat height-wise before they hit the max weight</strong>. An 80 lb seat is&hellip; let&rsquo;s just say I&rsquo;d be shocked to see an 11 year old (~80lb) child riding around in a convertible car seat&hellip; gimme a break. So, unless your kid is super heavy, you&rsquo;re never going to hit the upper weight limit &ndash; and if you did, your seat will have expired anyway! Hence, a 50-60lb seat should be sufficient for the vast majority of the population.</p>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;">All the Hubbub about Rear-Facing </span></p>
<p>If you are good boys and girls (which I know you are, ahem), you know you should be using your convertible seat <strong>rear-facing</strong> until at least 2 year of age (or longer, if you can stand it).</p>
<p>- A child is 75% more likely to sustain a serious injury if he is forward facing.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><span class="full-image-float-left ssNonEditable"><img src="/storage/carseats/front%20crash.png?__SQUARESPACE_CACHEVERSION=1355290124233" alt="" /></span>Yeah, yeah, yeah &ndash; I&rsquo;ve heard it -- he doesn&rsquo;t fit, you say? That&rsquo;s what everyone says.&nbsp;<strong>Remember</strong>: it doesn&rsquo;t matter that his legs are touching the back seat &ndash; he&rsquo;s NOT going to break his legs in an accident. If he&rsquo;s front-facing, however, he&rsquo;s way more likely to break his spinal cord which could kill or seriously maim him [see a great video&nbsp;<a title="YouTube Forward Facing vid" href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=psmUWg7QrC8" target="_blank">here</a>, cue to 1:30]. So,&nbsp;<em>I don&rsquo;t mean to be harsh</em>, but please, please, don&rsquo;t think you know more than the professionals who design and test these things for a living.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Having said that, here&rsquo;s the deal: rear-facing car seats are reclined (usually at 30-45 degrees) and therefore, <strong>take up a lot of space (i.e. depth) in your backseat </strong>&ndash; perhaps even more than your infant seat (yikes!). Therefore, space (i.e. will the damn thing fit?) is probably the #1 consideration.</p>
<p><strong><span style="color: black; font-size: 120%;">Methodology</span></strong></p>
<p>I&rsquo;ve said it before and I&rsquo;ll say it again, there is no &ldquo;best&rdquo; car seat out there. What fits into one car doesn&rsquo;t fit into another&hellip; what fits one kid doesn&rsquo;t fit another, blah blah bah. And if you thought choosing an infant car seat was confusing, JUST WAIT. Ha!</p>
<p><span style="color: black;">For simplicity sake, I picked the 5 best seats on the market (plus specialty seats), one for each price category (more or less). </span>I chose these seats because they give you the best value for your buck, in my personal opinion, based on experiences with my own car seats, my experiences as a CPST (Child Passenger Safety Technician), and taking into account hundreds of bits of feedback and user reviews.</p>
<p>BTW, it was REALLY HARD to pick just five seats. There are TONS of great seats out there, so just because a seat you love/own isn&rsquo;t on this list doesn&rsquo;t mean it&rsquo;s not great.</p>
<p>Also, because I feel very strongly about it, all of these seats offer at least <em>some</em> side-impact protection. Side-impact protection is especially important if your child is riding in one of the side or &ldquo;outboard&rdquo; positions (i.e. not in the middle). For more about where to put your seat, read <a title="Blog Post" href="http://www.lucieslist.com/lucies-list-blog/2012/5/14/real-world-car-seat-installation-tips.html" target="_blank">here</a>.</p>
<p>What we&rsquo;re aiming for here is the safest seat that fits 1) your car 2) your kid and 3) your wallet. &nbsp; It may not be what your neighbor or best friend has, so try not to get caught up in keeping up with the Joneses. &nbsp; Also remember -- <strong>there are no guarantees that the seat you buy will fit properly into your car</strong>.&nbsp; Be sure to keep the tags on (the box, wrapping, etc) so you can return it if it doesn&rsquo;t work. You&rsquo;ll be hating life if you get stuck with a seat that doesn&rsquo;t fit well.</p>
<p>*Note that all of the seats mentioned here are FAA approved to be brought aboard an airplane</p>
<p id="SUMMARY">For tips on installation, go <a title="Blog Post" href="http://www.lucieslist.com/lucies-list-blog/2012/5/14/real-world-car-seat-installation-tips.html" target="_blank">here</a>.</p>

<p>As you may (or may not) know, many people want to tether their convertible seat when its rear-facing to prevent what is called &ldquo;rebounding&rdquo;. Usually, tethering a rear-facing seat involves all kinds of shenanigans (the Swedish method, the Australian method) &ndash; ack! Seriously? Essentially, the built-in anti-rebound feature on this seat addresses this concern without having to screw with the tethering.&nbsp; If you are gung-ho about tethering your kid&rsquo;s seat while rear-facing, this is your dogg.</p>
