<p>Auburn, Ind.</p>
<p>It is a step back in time &#8212; way back in time. Plunked down in this town (city?) of about 14,000 in the northeast corner of Indiana, the art deco museum, once the headquarters for the Auburn Automobile Co., is a must-visit for any self-respecting car geek.</p>
<p>We are talking about cars that had their heyday more than half a century ago &#8212; the Auburn boat-tail Speedster, the Duesenberg J Murphy-bodied convertible &#8212; and to stroll about the museum&#8217;s six permanent galleries is to wander among cars that you really wish were being made today.</p>
<p>As it is, the closest you&#8217;ll get to something like this is to see them at Pebble Beach, or at an auction (from afar, as the guy next to you holds up his paddle and ratchets up the bid), or, if you make the trip, at this monument to a day long gone.</p>
<p>In fact, the day long gone began in 1900, when Charles Eckhart created the Auburn Automobile Co. The first car came out in 1903 and, over the next 20 years, or so, the company had a middling success that allowed it stay in business (modern car companies, take note), but was sold to a Chicago consortium in 1919.</p>
<p>Five years later, enter E.L. (Errett Lobban) Cord, a dynamic industrialist who was sort of a cross between Howard Hughes and Henry Ford. Cord was hired as &#8220;general manager, to salvage an inert company,&#8221; according to the museum, and he not only salvaged, he thrived, to use the modern idiom.</p>
<p>Cord realized that the whole operation needed a shot of adrenaline, a quick jolt of pizazz. In quick order, he hired pizazz-equipped designers like Alan Leamy and Gordon Buehrig. Between the two of them, they developed the elegant front-wheel-drive Cords, and the rear-wheel-drive Auburns and Duesenbergs that now grace the lawns at Amelia Island, Pebble Beach and other high-toned concours events.</p>
<p>The historically interesting part is that they once graced the driveways of movie stars, best-selling authors and those who, in the post-Depression 1930s, still had plenty of money to spend on, well, Duesenbergs.</p>
<p>Perhaps the first stop on your tour of the gallery, then, should be the 1932 Duesenberg Model J Murphy Torpedo convertible. It was owned, variously, by J. Paul Getty (think oil), writer John O&#8217;Hara (&#8220;Appointment in Samarra,&#8221; among other) and car guy Russell (Cliff) Durant, whose father founded General Motors.</p>
<p>Alas, this dazzling display of engineering acumen and styling talent would not last. As the museum&#8217;s own information brochure points out to visitors, &#8220;manufacturing ceased in 1937, due to changing tastes, hard economic times and internal corporate turmoil.&#8221;</p>
<p>On the other hand, their scarcity makes the cars even more desirable now and, when you walk around the museum, you&#8217;ll find yourself back in the 1930s, wondering if you could just take this boat-tail Speedster out for, oh, maybe ten minutes. Just around the block.</p>
<p>As I said earlier, this place is definitely worth a detour &#8212; I stopped at the museum during a recent cross-country drive from California to New England &#8212; and give yourself at least a couple of hours to soak it all in. It&#8217;s worth it.</p>
<p>(Text and photos by Michael Taylor)</p>
<p>The museum is at 1600 S. Wayne St., Auburn, Ind. 46706, Tel. (260) 925-1444. It&#8217;s open from 9 a.m. to 5 p.m. every day except Thanksgiving, Christmas and New Year&#8217;s. Here&#8217;s the museum&#8217;s Web site: www.automobilemuseum.org. (The mechanism for translating that into a link is on the fritz, so you&#8217;ll have to get there in a Duesenberg.)</p>

<p>The Gallants &#8212; Dan and Karen, of Gallant Racing Supply in Oakland &#8212; are the quintessential couple of the older car racing circuit. He builds the car, she builds the engine. He&#8217;s 67, she&#8217;s 69. The car is 21. (It&#8217;s a 1989 Pontiac Firebird whose only original shred of Pontiac is the skin of the roof and the taillights. Its engine pumps out about 650 horsepower and, on a good day, with the right gearing, it will do 172 miles an hour. Don&#8217;t try this at home.)</p>
<p>The Firebird was one of some 375 older race cars that took over Infineon Raceway in Sonoma County this past weekend (June 5 and 6) for the Sonoma Historic Motorsports Festival. The cars ranged in age from a 1914 Ford Racer up into brutally fast cars from the 1970s, and everything in between. This is a far cry from modern NASCAR racing or the Indy car circuit or Formula One, with cars being tuned by laptop computer and pit crews communicating with driver via radio. Not to mention the newish vogue of paddle-shifting.</p>
<p>Watching these Cobras, Corvettes, Elvas, Coopers, Lolas, Morgans, Aston Martins, Fords, Chevrolets, Sunbeam Tigers (and Alpines), Austin-Healeys, Alfa Romeos, Ferraris, Jaguars, Lotuses, Porsches and the odd TVR, Ginetta or Siata gambol about Infineon&#8217;s 2.52-mile track is, for most of the people here, a great, noisy, familiar orgy of high-octane fuel, higher-octane pumping of adrenaline and the overall knowledge that most of the drivers are older than younger, and they are not out to crash these expensive cars. Instead, they&#8217;re here to have fun, go fast and come back alive.</p>
<p>Why do they do it, when they could be sitting at home watching NASCAR races on the tube?</p>
<p>In 2004, Dan Payne retired as a captain with the Marin County Sheriff&#8217;s Office. Back in the early 1970s, he was just back from Vietnam. The sheriff&#8217;s office was looking for deputies for extra security during the San Quentin Six trial and he signed on. (Yes, that dates us, doesn&#8217;t it. Look it up. It&#8217;s part of Bay Area history.)</p>
<p>&#8220;I retired after 30 years in law enforcement,&#8221; he said, waiting for his turn to take his 1963 Corvette out on the track. &#8220;I was looking for a project.&#8221; He gestured at the car. &#8220;I love to restore them, and I love to drive them.&#8221;</p>
<p>He concedes that &#8220;there&#8217;s no prize money, no trophies, no kissing the race queen.&#8221; It doesn&#8217;t matter. He&#8217;s here because &#8220;it&#8217;s half car show, half racing.&#8221; And a lot of fun.</p>
<p>Of course, a lot of this auto idyll comes across better with photos. So here they are, in no particular order. (Text and photos by Michael Taylor.)</p>

<p>It&#8217;s always been one of my favorite stories about the car biz. Back in the early 1960s, Henry Ford II, grandson of the founder of Ford Motor Co., and one of the larger-than-life characters in an otherwise bland corporate world, decided he wanted to buy Ferrari.</p>
<p>Enzo Ferrari, no slouch when it came to manifestation of ego, said, fuhgeddaboutit.</p>
<p>Ford had this thing about Ferrari and his fabulous race cars and grand touring cars. Envy is a word that comes to mind.</p>
<p>So Ford did what comes naturally &#8212; when Ferrari snubbed him, he decided to beat Ferrari at his own game. He would build a car that would clean Ferrari&#8217;s clock at Le Mans.</p>
<p>And therein lies <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Go-Like-Hell-Ferrari-Battle/dp/0618822194/ref=sr_1_1?ie=UTF8&amp;s=books&amp;qid=1244655419&amp;sr=1-1" target="_blank">the tale </a>in <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2009/06/09/books/09baime.html?_r=1&amp;scp=1&amp;sq=charles%20mcgrath%20aj%20baime&amp;st=cse" target="_blank">a new book by A.J. Baime, &#8220;Go Like Hell: Ford, Ferrari and Their Battle for Speed and Glory at Le Mans.&#8221; </a>Ferrari had won Le Mans six years running (1960-1965). In 1966, having put Carroll Shelby in charge of its racing program, Ford won Le Mans, vanquishing Ferrari with a Ford GT40 sporting a monster 427-cubic-inch motor.</p>
<p>I mention all this because the Ford GT40 was always one of the cars I&#8217;ve lusted after for years. There were enough other GT40 lusters to convince Ford to make a streetgoing version of the car. It was produced in model years 2005 and 2006, and sold for about $150,000 each &#8212; I think Ford made fewer than 4,000 of them.</p>
<p><a href="http://www.sfgate.com/cgi-bin/article.cgi?f=/c/a/2006/06/16/MTGAOJE4VJ1.DTL" target="_blank">I drove one of the modern Ford GTs three years ago</a>. We had it for 48 hours and I think I spent 47 of them in the car. And, yes, it was not your ordinary Ford.</p>

<p>It&#8217;s one of those urban myths that floats around the car world, like the one about the guy who got an almost new Corvette for $100 because a.) somebody had recently died in it or b.) a wounded spouse was getting revenge on her mate during a divorce squabble and decided to sell his toy for as little as possible.</p>
<p>Then there&#8217;s the barn find story: guy retires and buys an old house on a property of several acres. Out back is a huge steel barn, its doors welded shut. Eventually, he dies, leaving it all to his heirs. Nobody has gone to the expense of seeing what&#8217;s in there, and the heirs don&#8217;t want to spend the money to open up the barn. Property is sold.</p>
<p>Inside the barn are &#8212; the count varies, as the story is spun out &#8212; 58 to 180 cars, all just sitting there, covered with dust, but otherwise okay.</p>
<p><a href="http://www.intuh.net/barnfinds/afa70.htm" target="_blank">Here&#8217;s where to start unraveling this story</a>. Turns out it&#8217;s true.</p>
<p>In <a href="http://www.sportscarmarket.com/articles/archives/1110" target="_blank">a piece by Tom Cotter</a>, in Keith Martin&#8217;s <a href="http://www.sportscarmarket.com/" target="_blank">Sports Car Market</a>, we learn that the cars are, indeed, in Portugal, as the original story had it. Cotter wrote:</p>
<p>&#8220;The owner of the cars was a car dealer in the 1970s and 1980s, and decided to save the more interesting cars that came through his doors. When the barn was full, he padlocked and &#8220;soldered&#8221; the doors shut. (Perhaps welding was too permanent.)</p>
<p>&#8220;Web sites varied on the number of cars: 58, 100, and 180 were speculated. According to Morais, there are 180 cars in the barn.</p>
<p>&#8220;And, aw shucks, none of the cars is for sale.&#8221;</p>

<p>&#8220;On an August morning in 1978, French filmmaker Claude Lelouch mounted a gyro-stabilized camera to the bumper of a Ferrari 275 GTB and had a friend, a professional Formula 1 racer, drive at breakneck speed through the heart of Paris early in the morning.</p>

<p>On Monday, we wrote about the three weather-beaten cars that sit at the west end of the Caltrans Bay Bridge parking lot. We mused about this motley collection of American autodom and promoted the sly opinion that maybe it was an outdoor exhibit of found art (photo below).</p>
<p>Not true!</p>
<p> Caltrans&#8217; chief Bay Area region spokeswoman, Lauren Wonder, says this (and she clearly has a sense of humor):</p>
<p>&#8220;Thank you so much for your thoughtful column about an outdoor museum display of old cars. While the cars might not make Towe Ford Museum jealous, they are also not an outdoor display.</p>
<p>&#8220;The vehicles are used by Caltrans tow operators in Tow School training to give them a chance to practice their new skills in a safe environment off of the travel way. They were &#8220;donated&#8221; by current and previous Caltrans tow operators, and the signed pink slips are in the possession of one of our tow service supervisors. These vehicles are out of service in the category of &#8220;junk&#8221; and not road-ready nor serviceable.</p>
<p>&#8220;The vehicles do not and will never run again. They have far exceeded their normal useful life and our new tow operators are getting a little training on their last breath.&#8221;</p>
<p>All well and good. The bonus, of course, is that, for those of us who ply the Bay Bridge commute day in and day out, it&#8217;s still an outdoor exhibit of interesting artifacts from the past century. The comments section of my original blog item had a surfeit of opinions  from the usual suspects, the ones who adhere to the principle of never having an unpublished thought, no matter its content. But there were also a couple of reasonable and constructive remarks by some people who put into focus my thoroughly hazy ID of the three cars.</p>
<p>So here&#8217;s what they are: mid-80s Chevy Camaro (at least, I got that one right); early 1970s AMC Hornet wagon (the yellow one on the right); and either a mid-80s Ford LTD Crown Victoria or 1978-83 Ford Fairmont.</p>
<p>Postscript: The consensus among the Commenterati is that it&#8217;s a Ford Fairmont, not a Crown Vic.</p>
<p>Post-postscript: a couple of days after the original post went up (Monday, April 13, 2009), Caltrans removed the cars. &#8220;We moved them out of people&#8217;s view,&#8221; says Lauren Wonder, Caltrans&#8217; chief Bay Area spokeswoman. They&#8217;re gone.</p>
<div class="postimageleft" style="width:500px"><img alt="The Caltrans Collection on a sunny day. <p>It must be the envy of curators around the world. It&#8217;s an outdoor exhibit (come rain or shine) that is seen by at least 1 million visitors a week and they each pay $4 for the privilege. That&#8217;s 52 million visitors a year!</p>
<p>Sometimes the patrons go by the collage at 60 miles an hour. Other times, they&#8217;re stopped (at the metering lights) long enough to contemplate the exhibit for minutes on end.</p>
<p>What they see, at the far end of the Caltrans parking lot west of the Bay Bridge toll plaza, is this:</p>
<div class="postimageleft" style="width:500px"><img alt="A dreamy exhibit of American automotive might. From left to right -- General Motors, Ford, American Motors" src="http://www.sfgate.com/blogs/images/sfgate/topdown/2009/04/10/CLUNKERS2500x375.jpg" width="500" height="375" border="0" /></p>
<p class="source">Michael Taylor</p>
<p class="caption">A dreamy exhibit of American automotive might (not counting the white Caltrans truck). From left to right &#8212; General Motors, Ford, American Motors</p>
</div>
<p>By my reckoning, these clunkers have been sitting there for at least a year. Who do they belong to? Or, maybe, who <em>did</em> they belong to?</p>
<p>What happened to them? Were they bridge wrecks that just fetched up on this distant Caltrans shore and, like the withering hulks of long-forgotten sailing ships, were simply ignored?</p>
<p>Clearly, they haven&#8217;t been running for a while and, just as clearly, they ain&#8217;t going to run for a long, long while, if ever.</p>
<p>My cursory ID of these poor souls is, roughly: mid-&#8217;80s Chevy Camaro; mid-&#8217;80s Ford of some stripe (Fairlane?); and, a true rarity, given that its parent company long ago went out of business, an early &#8217;80s (1980) AMC wagon, possibly a Concord.</p>
<p>So, Caltrans, somebody in your administration has obviously come up with a brilliant idea &#8212; make it a museum exhibit of found art and leave it there for the ages.</p>

<p>Ah, the difficulty of it all. You want to take your <a href="http://www.ddavid.com/formula1/lotus79.htm" target="_blank">Lotus Type 79 </a>Formula One car out for a ride at the local track, or you might want to just exercise your streetgoing Ferrari/Porsche/Lamborghini.</p>
<p>That means a trek to the track, hauling the race car, or driving the Lambo through those awful city streets, crawling along at 8 mph.</p>
<p>Well, you could always move to Agate, Colo. Where? Agate, southeast of Denver, where the Motor Sports Country Club of Colorado is developing the car geek&#8217;s ultimate live-in resort, replete with race track, clubhouse and restaurant.</p>
<p>The club will have some 50 homesites, on which you can build a house that fits within the organization&#8217;s architectural guidelines, according to a <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2009/01/30/greathomesanddestinations/30break1.html?_r=1&amp;scp=1&amp;sq=agate,%20colo&amp;st=cse" target="_blank">New York Times story</a>. A homesite will be about 35 acres, which should give you ample room to park your stable of toys.</p>
<p>The club&#8217;s <a href="http://www.motorsportcc.com/" target="_blank">Web site </a>was a bit balky when I tried it a few days ago. You can also reach them at (303) 459-7745.</p>

<p>The U.S. auto industry is taking it on the chin and just about every other body part these days, but there was a time when the products of Detroit were highly coveted cars that took on a new life once they got in the hands of the local hot rod shop.</p>
<p>Ford flatheads, the Chevy 265-cubic-inch V8, Chrysler hemi engines, tail fins, chopped and channeled &#8212; they&#8217;re terms that are foreign to many these days.</p>
<p>For those to whom they&#8217;re not foreign, or for those who want to find out more about all this, the best place to learn might well be the <a href="http://www.sfcustomshow.com/navigation/home.html#Anchor-Time-47857" target="_blank">San Francisco Rod, Custom and Motorcycle Show,</a> this Friday (noon to 10 p.m.), Saturday (9 a.m. to 10 p.m.) and Sunday (9 a.m. to 7 p.m.), at the Cow Palace in San Francisco.(Feb. 20-22).</p>
<p>Among other cars, you&#8217;ll see the 1954 Cadillac coupe belonging to Oakland Raiders offensive lineman Robert Galley and a highly modified 1946 Diamond T five-ton big bed truck &#8212; not for just hauling out the scraps.</p>
