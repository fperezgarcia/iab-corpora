				<img width="150" height="150" src="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/wp-content/uploads/OngBak1-150x1501.jpg" class="thumb  wp-post-image" alt="Top 20 Martial Arts Films of All Time" title="Top 20 Martial Arts Films of All Time" />        
<p>Twenty-first century <a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/category/kung-fu/">kung fu</a> film fans are more aware and appreciative of good movies than their ancestors ever were. There are three main reason for this: the mainstream success of Chinese-language martial arts films such as Ang Lees <em>Crouching Tiger, Hidden Dragon</em> and Zhang Yimous <em>Hero</em> and <em>House of Flying Daggers</em> in the West; the use of stylized Hong Kong action in Hollywood blockbusters; and the international success of <a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/category/jackie-chan-movies/">Jackie Chan</a>, <a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/category/jet-li-movies/">Jet Li</a>, Michelle Yeoh and their cohorts.</p>
<hr />
<p style="text-align: center;"><em>Learn even more martial arts movie trivia with this FREE Guide!<br />
<a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/freeguides/our-bruce-lee-movies-list-little-known-trivia-from-bruce-lees-pictures/">Our Bruce Lee Movies List: Little-Known Trivia From Bruce Lee&#8217;s Pictures</a>.</em></p>
<hr />
Because of their current status in the American martial arts community, <em>Black Belt </em>asked me to devise a list of the top 20 <a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/category/martial-art-movies/">martial arts movies</a> of all time. The result is this article. Movies were included based on their impact on martial arts cinema, as well as their choreography. </p>
<p>Before jumping into the list, however, Ill note a few honorable mentions: </p>
<ul class="bullet">
<li><em>Crouching Tiger</em> for giving small-town America a view of the <em>wu xia</em> world of martial arts films</li>
<li><em>Kung Fu vs. Yoga</em> for &#8230; well, you just cant ignore a guy whos capable of whipping his right leg up across his back and kicking over his left shoulder to strike the face of a man standing in front of him</li>
<li><em>Magnificent Butcher</em> for whats arguably Sammo Hungs best fight performance</li>
<li><em>The Sword of Doom</em> for being the best samurai film ever made</li>
<li><em>Once Upon a Time in China</em> for resurrecting the legend of Huang Fei-hung, for defining Jet Li and for introducing the world to the Hong Kong kick</li>
<li><em>Armor of Gods</em> for featuring in its final fight four of Jackie Chans skinny Chinese stuntmen doubling four large-bosomed black women. Youve got to love it.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Martial Arts Movie #20 &#8211; One-Armed Swordsman (1967)</h2>
<div>
<div id="attachment_2106" class="wp-caption alignleft" style="width: 160px"><a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/wp-content/uploads/One-ArmedSwordsman.jpg"><img class="size-thumbnail wp-image-2106" title="One-ArmedSwordsman" src="/wp-content/uploads/One-ArmedSwordsman-150x150.jpg" alt="One-Armed Swordsman (1967)" width="150" height="150" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">One-Armed Swordsman (1967)</p></div>
<p>At a time when musicals and romances overshadowed action films and women ruled the Hong Kong silver screen, legendary director Chang Cheh burst onto the scene with <em>One-Armed Swordsman.</em> This riveting revenge thriller, filled with themes of heroic bloodshed and violence, reversed the Cantonese and Mandarin starlet-entrenched cinematic trends. It was also a pivotal transition between <em>wu xia</em> movies and kung fu films, and it introduced the world to the stoically charismatic Jimmy Wong Yu.</p>
</div>
<h2>Martial Arts Movie #19 &#8211; Ong-Bak (2003)</h2>
<div>
<div id="attachment_2109" class="wp-caption alignleft" style="width: 160px"><a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/wp-content/uploads/OngBak1.jpg"><img class="size-thumbnail wp-image-2109" title="OngBak" src="/wp-content/uploads/OngBak1-150x150.jpg" alt="Ong-Bak (2003)" width="150" height="150" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Ong-Bak (2003)</p></div>
<p>This Thai Film Festival award winner starring <a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/category/tony-jaa-movies/">Tony Jaa</a> (Robin Shous stunt double in <em><a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/tag/mortal-kombat/">Mortal Kombat</a>: Annihilation)</em> is not only the first martial arts flick to feature a highly stylized version of Thai kickboxing, but the bone-crunching stunts and full-contact body attacks are also a noncomedic throwback to Jackie Chans films from the mid-1980s. It hurts to watch this movie, but it brings back great memories of why we used to love Hong Kong action.</p>
</div>
<h2>Martial Arts Movie #18 &#8211; Legend of the Fox (1979)</h2>
<div>
<div id="attachment_2111" class="wp-caption alignleft" style="width: 160px"><a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/wp-content/uploads/LegendoftheFox.jpg"><img class="size-thumbnail wp-image-2111" title="LegendoftheFox" src="/wp-content/uploads/LegendoftheFox-150x150.jpg" alt="Legend of the Fox (1979)" width="150" height="150" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Legend of the Fox (1979)</p></div>
<p>After acclaimed director Chang Cheh made <em>The Five Venoms</em> (1978), he made 18 other films with the same actors, in which each took turns being the villain, the hero and the fight choreographer. Any of those 18 movies could occupy this spot, but <em>Legend of the Fox</em> gets the nod for its far-out, ultra-intricate pugilistic scenes and weapon sequences, as well as its strict adherence to the true brotherhood thats supposed to exist among martial artists  even those who are adversaries. Chang is one of the very few martial arts directors who consistently captured this spirit.</p>
</div>
<h2>Martial Arts Movie #17 &#8211; Three Evil Masters (1980)</h2>
<div>
<div id="attachment_2113" class="wp-caption alignleft" style="width: 160px"><a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/wp-content/uploads/ThreeEvilMasters0001.jpg"><img class="size-thumbnail wp-image-2113" title="ThreeEvilMasters0001" src="/wp-content/uploads/ThreeEvilMasters0001-150x150.jpg" alt="Three Evil Masters (1980)" width="150" height="150" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Three Evil Masters (1980)</p></div>
<p>Although <em>Masters</em> stars the legendary Chen Kuan-tai, fans are in for a real treat with the film debut of Yuen Tak playing the downtrodden kung fu student Ko Chien. Yuen is Jackie Chans kung fu classmate, and according to fellow classmate Corey Yuen, Yuen Taks kung fu skills are considered the best among the Seven Little Fortunes. The final fights are sharply intense and brilliantly staged, especially the weapons battle between Yuen Tak and Shaw Brothers perennial villain Wang Lung-wei.</p>
</div>
<h2>Martial Arts Movie #16 &#8211; Prodigal Son (1981)</h2>
<div>
<div id="attachment_2115" class="wp-caption alignleft" style="width: 160px"><a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/wp-content/uploads/ProdigalSon0001.jpg"><img class="size-thumbnail wp-image-2115" title="ProdigalSon0001" src="/wp-content/uploads/ProdigalSon0001-150x150.jpg" alt="Prodigal Son (1981)" width="150" height="150" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Prodigal Son (1981)</p></div>
<p>Directed by Sammo Hung, this learn-martial-arts-for-revenge film is considered the best <a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/category/wing-chun/"><em>wing chun</em></a> movie ever. Although it stars Hung and Yuen Biao, its the ambiguous, stalwart performance of and use of wing chun by the late Lam Ching-ying that steal the show as he effortlessly glides through each fight scene like an Olympic ice skater. Lams female opera-character performance is also outstanding.
</div>
<p><P>&nbsp;<P></p>
<h2>Martial Arts Movie #15 &#8211; The Shaolin Temple (1980)</h2>
<div>
<div id="attachment_2117" class="wp-caption alignleft" style="width: 160px"><a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/wp-content/uploads/ShaolinTemple0001.jpg"><img class="size-thumbnail wp-image-2117" title="ShaolinTemple0001" src="/wp-content/uploads/ShaolinTemple0001-150x150.jpg" alt="The Shaolin Temple (1980)" width="150" height="150" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">The Shaolin Temple (1980)</p></div>
<p>Based on a semi-true story of how <a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/category/shaolin-kung-fu/">Shaolin</a> monks rescued the first emperor of the Tang dynasty, <em>The Shaolin Temple</em> was not only Jet Lis cinematic debut but also the first martial arts epic made in mainland China since the Communist takeover. The film was instrumental in introducing <em>wushu</em> to film fans around the world. However, its most important impact was the reinstatement of the cultural value and historicity of Shaolin Temple.
</div>
<p><P>&nbsp;<P></p>
<h2>Martial Arts Movie #14 &#8211; Drunken Master (1978)</h2>
<div>
<div id="attachment_2119" class="wp-caption alignleft" style="width: 160px"><a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/wp-content/uploads/DrunkenMaster0001.jpg"><img class="size-thumbnail wp-image-2119" title="DrunkenMaster0001" src="/wp-content/uploads/DrunkenMaster0001-150x150.jpg" alt="Drunken Master (1978)" width="150" height="150" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Drunken Master (1978)</p></div>
<p>Its considered the vehicle that launched Jackie Chan on the path to superstardom. When Golden Harvest realized Jackie Chan couldnt, wouldnt and shouldnt be the next Bruce Lee, they teamed him up with director Yuen Woo-ping to create the first film to show drunken <a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/category/kung-fu/">kung fu</a>, which was taught to Jackie Chan by Yuens father, Yuen Xiao-tian. The senior Yuen also played Jackie Chans beggar teacher in the movie. Kung fu comedy was secured.<br />
<P>&nbsp;<P></p>
<hr />
<p style="text-align: center;"><em>Go behind the scenes of Bruce Lees final film with this FREE download!<br />
<a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/freeguides/bruce-lee-movies-the-making-of-enter-the-dragon/">Bruce Lee Movies: The Making of Enter the Dragon</a>.</em></p>
<hr />
</div>
<p><P>&nbsp;<P></p>
<h2>Martial Arts Movie #13 &#8211; The Blade (1993)</h2>
<div>
<div id="attachment_2121" class="wp-caption alignleft" style="width: 160px"><a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/wp-content/uploads/Blade.jpg"><img class="size-thumbnail wp-image-2121" title="Blade" src="/wp-content/uploads/Blade-150x150.jpg" alt="Blade (1993)" width="150" height="150" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">The Blade (1993)</p></div>
<p>Although not well-received by Hong Kong audiences, Tsui Harks visceral retelling of <em>One-Armed Swordsman</em> is an engaging, violent, revenge-begetting-revenge story starring the enigmatic Zhao Wen-zhuo (the man who replaced Jet Li in Tsuis <em>Once Upon a Time in China</em> films) battling the maniacal Xiong Xin-xin. Fight director Yuen Bings action is a dazzling display of clanging swords, swirling flames, billowing smoke and gut-wrenching sequences of flesh hacking and whacking that will leave you breathless.</p>
</div>
<h2>Martial Arts Movie #12 &#8211; Zu: Warriors From Magic Mountain (1982)</h2>
<div>
<div id="attachment_2043" class="wp-caption alignleft" style="width: 160px"><a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/wp-content/uploads/Zu.jpg"><img class="size-thumbnail wp-image-2043" title="Zu" src="/wp-content/uploads/Zu-150x150.jpg" alt="Zu: Warriors From Magic Mountain (1982)" width="150" height="150" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Zu: Warriors From Magic Mountain (1982)</p></div>
<p>This movie started the fant-Asia film craze, a mixture of horror, fantasy and science fiction with over-the-top martial arts action. It also initiated the now-prominent wire-fu action as created by Hong Kongs Ching Siu-tung. <em>Zu</em> gracefully intertwines Chinese myth, brilliant special effects and comic-book action. Its a roller coaster of magic, giant world-protector deities, killer poltergeists and supernatural heroes sporting phenomenal fighting skills.</p>
</div>
<p><P>&nbsp;<P></p>
<h2>Martial Arts Movie #11 &#8211; Moon Warriors (1993)</h2>
<div>
<div id="attachment_2124" class="wp-caption alignleft" style="width: 160px"><a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/wp-content/uploads/MoonWarriors0001.jpg"><img class="size-thumbnail wp-image-2124" title="MoonWarriors0001" src="/wp-content/uploads/MoonWarriors0001-150x150.jpg" alt="Moon Warriors (1993)" width="150" height="150" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Moon Warriors (1993)</p></div>
<p>Ching Siu-tung is renowned for making non-martial artists look like the best fighters and swordsmen in the history of mankind. That skill is never more evident than in <em>Moon Warriors,</em> a film filled with Canto-pop stars such as Anita Mui, Maggie Cheung and Andy Lau. Ching works steel slashing into pure magic, and just when you think Hong Kong movies cant get any more outrageous, theres a fight scene featuring a real killer whale.</p>
</div>
<p><P>&nbsp;<P></p>
<h2>Martial Arts Movie #10 &#8211; Police Story (1985)</h2>
<div>
<div id="attachment_2127" class="wp-caption alignleft" style="width: 160px"><a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/wp-content/uploads/PoliceStory00011.jpg"><img class="size-thumbnail wp-image-2127" title="PoliceStory0001" src="/wp-content/uploads/PoliceStory00011-150x150.jpg" alt="Police Story (1985)" width="150" height="150" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Police Story (1985)</p></div>
<p>Just when Hong Kong martial arts movies were all beginning to look the same, Jackie Chan came along with <em>Police Story.</em> It gave the industry a face-lift by creating a whole new style of martial arts movies called <em>wu da pian</em> &mdash; a genre that mixed contemporary themes, fast-paced choreography, and the most dangerous and amazing stunts ever put on film. With no wires, no doubles and no holding back, <em>Police Story</em> cemented Jackie Chan as one of the worlds greatest stuntmen.</p>
</div>
<h2>Martial Arts Movie #9 &#8211; <a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/daily/martial-arts-entertainment/martial-art-movies/tai-chi-master-review-vintage-jet-li-films/">Tai-Chi Master</a> (1993)</h2>
<div>
<div id="attachment_2129" class="wp-caption alignleft" style="width: 160px"><a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/wp-content/uploads/TaichiMaster0001.jpg"><img class="size-thumbnail wp-image-2129" title="TaichiMaster0001" src="/wp-content/uploads/TaichiMaster0001-150x150.jpg" alt="Tai Chi Master (1993)" width="150" height="150" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Tai-Chi Master (1993)</p></div>
<p>Although Jet Li will be remembered mostly for portraying folk hero Wong Fei-hung in the <em>Once Upon a Time in China</em> series, its his role as <em>tai chi</em> founder Chang San-feng (also spelled Zhang San-feng) in <em>Tai-Chi Master</em> that flaunts his best fighting performance to date. Directed by Yuen Woo-ping and featuring some superb fighting from Michelle Yeoh, this movie would have done much better a few years earlier because at the time of its release, the style of film had run its course.</p>
</div>
<h2>Martial Arts Movie #8 &#8211; Martial Club (1980)</h2>
<div>
<div id="attachment_2131" class="wp-caption alignleft" style="width: 160px"><a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/wp-content/uploads/MartialClub0001.jpg"><img class="size-thumbnail wp-image-2131" title="MartialClub0001" src="/wp-content/uploads/MartialClub0001-150x150.jpg" alt="Martial Club (1980)" width="150" height="150" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Martial Club (1980)</p></div>
<p>Liu Chia-liang  whose first directed film <em>Spiritual Boxer</em> (1978) started the kung fu-comedy genre  helmed <em>Martial Club,</em> which stars Gordon Liu Chia-hui as Huang Fei-hong and includes a rare appearance by Wang Lung-wei as a nonvillain. The final conflict involving those two  which takes place within the confines of a 3-foot-wide alley and without wires  beautifully depicts the effectiveness of <em>hung gar</em> kung fu in tight spaces. This fight inspired Jet Lis bathroom battle sequence in his latest film, <em>Unleashed.</em></p>
</div>
<h2>Martial Arts Movie #7 &#8211; Duel to the Death (1983)</h2>
<div>
<div id="attachment_2133" class="wp-caption alignleft" style="width: 160px"><a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/wp-content/uploads/DuelTotheDeath00011.jpg"><img class="size-thumbnail wp-image-2133" title="DuelTotheDeath0001" src="/wp-content/uploads/DuelTotheDeath00011-150x150.jpg" alt="Duel to the Death (1983)" width="150" height="150" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Duel to the Death (1983)</p></div>
<p>Directed by the father of wire-fu, Ching Siu-tung, this film blurs the line between the real and the supernatural. The villains are ninja who seem neither human nor poltergeist. Chings swordplay action is said by some to have honed decapitation into a fine art and by others to have plunged human vegematics to the depths of beauteous slaughter. No one has ever done a ninja film that comes close to this one.</p>
</div>
<p><P>&nbsp;<P></p>
<h2>Martial Arts Movie #6 &#8211; Swordsman II (1992)</h2>
<div>
<div id="attachment_2135" class="wp-caption alignleft" style="width: 160px"><a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/wp-content/uploads/SwordsmanII0001.jpg"><img class="size-thumbnail wp-image-2135" title="SwordsmanII0001" src="/wp-content/uploads/SwordsmanII0001-150x150.jpg" alt="Swordsman II (1992)" width="150" height="150" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Swordsman II (1992)</p></div>
<p>Starring Jet Li as the title character, this mesmerizing film from director Ching Siu-tung gave creative carnage a new meaning while raising the sword-and-sorcery genre from the hollows of selfish individualism. It pays homage to the classic Chinese swordplay epics of yesteryear with imaginative special effects, meticulously choreographed fight scenes and acrobatics that defy the laws of gravity. Its a fine example of a movie that combines entertainment with spiritual substance, in which good battles evil and high-vaulting villains distinguish themselves as champions of the lowly. Chings <em>Swordsman I</em> and <em>Swordsman III</em> were also popular.</p>
</div>
<h2>Martial Arts Movie #5 &#8211; Come Drink With Me (1965)</h2>
<div>
<div id="attachment_2138" class="wp-caption alignleft" style="width: 160px"><a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/wp-content/uploads/ComeDrinkWithMe0001.jpg"><img class="size-thumbnail wp-image-2138" title="ComeDrinkWithMe0001" src="/wp-content/uploads/ComeDrinkWithMe0001-150x125.jpg" alt="Come Drink With Me (1965)" width="150" height="125" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Come Drink With Me (1965)</p></div>
<p>This classic directed by the legendary King Hu stars kung fus first screen queen, Cheng Pei-pei. She plays the mysterious swordswoman Golden Swallow, who tries to free a kidnapped official held in a Buddhist monastery. It set new standards for martial arts movies as its 20-year-old star became the most renowned kung fu heroine in martial arts cinema history. With fight direction by Hang Ying-jie (the big boss in Bruce Lees <em>The Big Boss), Come Drink With Me</em> inspired Ang Lees <em>Crouching Tiger, Hidden Dragon,</em> which is why Ang Lee insisted on casting Cheng as the treacherous Jade Fox.</p>
</div>
<h2>Martial Arts Movie #4 &#8211; Legendary Weapons of China (1982)</h2>
<div>
<div id="attachment_2140" class="wp-caption alignleft" style="width: 160px"><a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/wp-content/uploads/LegendaryWeaponsofKungFu.jpg"><img class="size-thumbnail wp-image-2140" title="LegendaryWeaponsofKungFu" src="/wp-content/uploads/LegendaryWeaponsofKungFu-150x150.jpg" alt="Legendary Weapons of China (1982)" width="150" height="150" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Legendary Weapons of China (1982)</p></div>
<p>The best pure martial arts director of all time, Liu Chia-liang stars and directs himself as Lei Gong, a Chinese boxer who quits an evil sect of martial artists who think they can stop bullets from Western guns. The final 10-minute fight between the 50-year-old Liu and his real brother, Liu Chia-rong, features the most outstanding and authentic Chinese weapons choreography in the history of the genre. Each weapon is clearly demonstrated in one continuous scene that you wish would never end.</p>
</div>
<p><P>&nbsp;<P></p>
<h2>Martial Arts Movie #3 &#8211; The 36th Chamber of Shaolin (1977)</h2>
<div>
<div id="attachment_2141" class="wp-caption alignleft" style="width: 160px"><a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/wp-content/uploads/36thChamberofShaolin1.jpg"><img class="size-thumbnail wp-image-2141" title="36thChamberofShaolin" src="/wp-content/uploads/36thChamberofShaolin1-150x150.jpg" alt="The 36th Chamber of Shaolin (1977)" width="150" height="150" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">The 36th Chamber of Shaolin (1977)</p></div>
<p>Liu Chia-liang directs his adoptive brother, Gordon Liu Chia-hui, as an anti-Ching-dynasty rebel in the first Shaolin film to reveal the secret training methods of the temple. This award-winning work changed the direction of all subsequent Shaolin movies as it chronicled Gordon Liu Chia-huis character as it changes from a simple-minded, unskilled fighter into one of Shaolins greatest real-life heroes, monk San Te. <em>The 36th Chamber</em> also signaled the start of a new genre of martial arts films known as <em>guo shu pian,</em> which was solidified by <em>The Five Venoms.</em></p>
</div>
<h2>Martial Arts Movie #2 &#8211; The Chinese Connection (1972)</h2>
<div>
<div id="attachment_2142" class="wp-caption alignleft" style="width: 160px"><a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/wp-content/uploads/ChineseConnection.jpg"><img class="size-thumbnail wp-image-2142" title="ChineseConnection" src="/wp-content/uploads/ChineseConnection-150x150.jpg" alt="Chinese Connection (1972)" width="150" height="150" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">The Chinese Connection (1972)</p></div>
<p><a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/category/bruce-lee/">Bruce Lee</a> rocketed into superstardom with this movie. It sparked a tidal wave of nationalism among the worlds Chinese population as its hero single-handedly defeats the shadow of Japanese domination. Loosely based on the real-life death of the legendary martial artist Huo Yuan-chia, the scene in which Bruce Lee kicks seven opponents in one unedited shot in a Japanese karate school altered the direction of fight choreography forever.</p>
</div>
<p><P>&nbsp;<P></p>
<h2>Martial Arts Movie #1 &#8211; Drunken Master II (1994)</h2>
<div>
<div id="attachment_2143" class="wp-caption alignleft" style="width: 160px"><a href="http://www.blackbeltmag.com/wp-content/uploads/DrunkenMasterII1.jpg"><img class="size-thumbnail wp-image-2143" title="DrunkenMasterII" src="/wp-content/uploads/DrunkenMasterII1-150x150.jpg" alt="Drunken Master II (1994)" width="150" height="150" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Drunken Master II (1994)</p></div>
<p>Jackie Chan reprises his portrayal of the legendary Huang Fei-hung in this sequel, which far surpasses the 1978 original. He battles traitors smuggling Chinese historical artifacts into the hands of the British. The final brawl with Ken Lo in the warehouse and the spear-and-sword fight with 60-year-old actor/director Liu Chia-liang under a train once and for all show why Jackie Chan is the best when it comes to using his environment in cinematic battles.
</p></div>
