<div class="entry-content">
<div id="asset-10525223" class="entry_widget_large entry_widget_right"><span class="adv-photo-large"><img height="252" width="380" src="http://media.mlive.com/auto_impact/photo/10525223-large.jpg" class="adv-photo" alt="Mike-Tinskey.JPG"><span class="photo-data"><a href="http://media.mlive.com/auto_impact/photo/mike-tinskeyjpg-bd163b5b98ceded7.jpg" target="_blank" class="full-size-popup">View full size</a><span class="byline">AutomotiveWorld.com</span><span class="caption">Mike Tinskey, associate director for Ford's Global Electric Vehicle Infrastructure, speaks Feb. 2 at the Automotive Megatrends USA 2012 conference in Dearborn.</span></span><span class="photo-bottom-left"><!-- IE6 HACK --></span><span class="photo-bottom-right"><!-- IE6 HACK --></span></span></div><p>Think back 10 years -- gasoline was under $1.50 a gallon and a fairly new device called an <a href="http://www.apple.com/pr/products/ipodhistory/">iPod could hold up to 4,000 songs</a> &#8220;in your pocket.&#8221;</p><p>Now -- <a href="http://www.mlive.com/auto/index.ssf/2012/02/what_does_next_decade_hold_for.html">think ahead 10 years</a>. What do you see? </p><p>While it is impossible to know what exactly the future holds, automotive industry executives and analysts recently took a shot at envisioning what the future holds for electric vehicles.</p><p>&#8220;We think this is more of a marathon than a sprint,&#8221; said Mike Tinskey, associate director for <a href="http://www.mlive.com/auto/index.ssf/2012/02/official_ford_focus_electric_s.html">Ford Motor Co.'s</a> Global Electric Vehicle Infrastructure. </p><p>Tinskey was one of nearly 60 speakers at the <a href="http://www.mlive.com/auto/index.ssf/2012/02/what_does_next_decade_hold_for.html">Automotive Megatrends USA 2012 </a>conference last week in Dearborn. More than 330 people attended the three-day event -- hosted by United Kingdom-based<a href="http://www.automotiveworld.com/"> AutmotiveWorld.com</a> -- to discuss numerous issues facing the automotive industry, as well as the reality of electric vehicles, with .</p><p>While there were varying opinions regarding electric vehicles -- plug-in electric and all-electric -- two ideas were nearly universal: Costs need to decline for the vehicles to become mainstream, and electric vehicles will remain a niche market for years to come.</p><p>According to Thilo Koslowski, <a href="http://www.gartner.com/technology/home.jsp">Gartner </a>vice president and lead automotive analyst, the 18,000 electric vehicles sold in 2011 accounted for about 0.14 percent of the market. He said by the end of this year automakers are hopeful that the market share will grow to 0.74 -- about 100,000 vehicles.</p><div id="asset-9766062" class="entry_widget_large entry_widget_left"><span class="adv-photo-large"><img height="253" width="380" src="http://media.mlive.com/auto_impact/photo/9766062-large.jpg" class="adv-photo" alt="Leaf-Volt-charging.jpg"><span class="photo-data"><a href="http://media.mlive.com/auto_impact/photo/leaf-volt-chargingjpg-cfd0b09e56b9391f.jpg" target="_blank" class="full-size-popup">View full size</a><span class="byline">AP file photo</span><span class="caption">A Nissan Leaf (left) and a Chevy Volt (right) are charged during a demo at the first ever quick charge electric vehicle charging station in the state of Texas, located at the Walgreens at Beltline and Monfort, which was unveiled in Dallas on Friday, April 8, 2011. It is one of dozens of new stations NRG Energy Inc. and others are planing for Dallas and Houston.</span></span><span class="photo-bottom-left"><!-- IE6 HACK --></span><span class="photo-bottom-right"><!-- IE6 HACK --></span></span></div><p>&#8220;That&#8217;s a pretty tiny share,&#8221; he said via webex. &#8220;But this is a fairly new market, so it&#8217;s not unheard of.&#8221;</p><p>Koslowski, who believes it will be an impressive year if sales hit 30,000-40,000 units, said that although the numbers are small, it is not uncommon for new technologies to take decades to catch on.</p><p>&#8220;Electric service (household electricity) took 50 years to get into all the homes out there in the U.S.,&#8221; said Earl Bloom, <a href="http://www.dowkokam.com/index.php">Dow Kokam</a> Global Sales director. &#8220;You think it would have been a lot quicker.</p><p>&#8220;So again, we have to look at infrastructure, cost and means.&#8221;</p><p>Bloom said as production of electric vehicles continues to grow; the cost will decline, which in turn, will allow the vehicles to be more affordable to the masses.</p><p>Bloom said a more recent example of technology adoption is personal computers, which remained a niche market less than 20 years ago.</p><p>&#8220;These are relatively simple technologies compared to electric cars and trucks,&#8221; he said.</p><p>According to Axeon Power Director of Business Development North America Michael Muzzin, electric vehicles, such as the plug-in hybrid electric <a href="http://topics.mlive.com/tag/Chevrolet%20Volt/index.html">Chevrolet Volt</a>, have more than 300 major components, compared to advanced gasoline engines, such as the <a href="http://www.autoblog.com/2007/06/14/video-bugatti-veyrons-w16-engine-being-milled/">Bugatti Veyron W16 Engine</a>, that have around 100.</p><div id="asset-9050157" class="entry_widget_large entry_widget_right"><span class="adv-photo-large"><img height="233" width="380" src="http://media.mlive.com/auto_impact/photo/9050157-large.jpg" class="adv-photo" alt="ford-focus-electric-schematic.jpg"><span class="photo-data"><a href="http://media.mlive.com/auto_impact/photo/ford-focus-electric-schematicjpg-ea03182b24b1d0fb.jpg" target="_blank" class="full-size-popup">View full size</a><span class="byline">Ford Motor Co.</span><span class="caption">A look at the workings of the Ford Focus Electric.</span></span><span class="photo-bottom-left"><!-- IE6 HACK --></span><span class="photo-bottom-right"><!-- IE6 HACK --></span></span></div><p>Muzzin said one of the first steps in lowering the cost of electric vehicles is to cut down on the parts, which can include more than 280 in the battery cells alone.</p><p>&#8220;We&#8217;re still seeking out those best ideas and those best technologies,&#8221; he said, adding a better standardization and regulation of the industry would help reduce complexity, and increase integration. &#8220;The answers haven&#8217;t been found yet because we&#8217;re not even at the point where we are asking the right questions yet.&#8221;</p><p>Officials said those &#8220;right questions&#8221; will come, as more automakers release new technologies and electric vehicles.  By 2013, the number of electric vehicles being offered in the U.S. are expected to grow from eight to 20.</p><p>Automakers, such as Ford, <a href="http://www.mlive.com/auto/index.ssf/2012/02/official_ford_focus_electric_s.html">are diversifying their lineups</a> to include more electric vehicles.&nbsp;</p><p>About 1 percent of Ford&#8217;s fleet in 2011 was is electrified, including the Ford Transit Connect Electric. By 2020, the automaker anticipates to have 10 to 25 percent of its global fleet electrified with plug-in or hybrid technology. Of that 2020 lineup, Ford expects hybrids to be 70 percent of the fleet, hybrid-plug-ins to be 20-25 percent and all-electric vehicles to remain a niche market of about 5 percent.</p><p><i>For more information on the Automotive Megatrends USA 2012 conference, visit <a href="http://www.AutomotiveWorld.com">AutomotiveWorld.com</a>.</i></p><p><i><font class="Apple-style-span" style="margin-top: 0px; margin-right: 0px; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-left: 0px; padding-top: 0px; padding-right: 0px; padding-bottom: 0px; padding-left: 0px; font-size: 0.8em; ">Email&nbsp;<a href="http://connect.mlive.com/user/mwayland/posts.html" style="margin-top: 0px; margin-right: 0px; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-left: 0px; padding-top: 0px; padding-right: 0px; padding-bottom: 0px; padding-left: 0px; cursor: pointer; ">Michael Wayland</a>: MWayland@mlive.com and follow him on Twitter at&nbsp;<a href="https://twitter.com/#%21/MikeWayland" style="margin-top: 0px; margin-right: 0px; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-left: 0px; padding-top: 0px; padding-right: 0px; padding-bottom: 0px; padding-left: 0px; cursor: pointer; ">twitter.com/MikeWayland</a></font></i></p>
