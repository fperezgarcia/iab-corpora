              <h1 align="center"><font size="7">Hydrogen Fuel Cell Cars</font></h1>
              <p>Hydrogen fuel cell cars are quite simply the future of the automotive 
                industry. These vehicles are electric vehicles that use a <a href="hydrogen-fuel-cells.htm">hydrogen 
                fuel cell</a> for power instead of a battery pack. </p>
              <table width="656" cellspacing="2" cellpadding="2" height="188">
                <tr>
                  <td width="482">
                    <div align="center"> 
                      <script type="text/javascript"><!--

              <p>Most of the major automobile manufacturers today have one or 
                more hydrogen fuel cell cars in their lineup of prototypes. Some, 
                carmakers like Ford have both hydrogen fuel cell vehicles and 
                H2ICE (hydrogen internal combustion engine) cars in their lineups. 
              </p>
              <p>Almost all of the H2 vehicles being shown today are built around 
                a hydrogen PEM (proton exchange membrane) fuel cell (Volkswagen 
                is one exception that has been experimenting with a solid oxide 
                fuel cell). </p>
              <p>A couple of the hydrogen fuel cell cars that have made headlines 
                of the past couple of years are the <a href="honda-fcx-clarity.htm">Honda 
                FCX Clarity</a> and the <a href="chevy-equinox-fuel-cell-suv.htm">Chevy 
                Equinox Fuel Cell</a>. The Honda Clarity has won numerous awards 
                including the 2009 World Green Car award and the 2009 Grove Medal.</p>
              <p>The Honda Clarity is also part of a pilot program where it is 
                being leased to select consumers for $600 per month over two years 
                time. Up to 200 Honda FCX Clarity's are to be leased and the results 
                studied by the company's engineers.</p>
              <p>The Chevy Equinox Fuel Cell, on the other hand, is part of the 
                GM Project Driveway Program. Project Driveway is putting the Chevy 
                Equinox Fuel Cell vehicle in the hands of over 200 drivers for 
                2 or 3 month periods for test driving.</p>
              <p>The drivers of these two fuel cell cars have to be at least in 
                the Los Angeles area where most of the public <a href="hydrogen-fuel-stations.htm">hydrogen 
                fueling stations</a> in the U. S. are currently located, though 
                Project Driveway members can also be in New York or Washington 
                DC which also have several H2 fueling stations at their disposal.</p>
              <h3><i>GM and Honda, Daimler ...</i></h3>
              <p>Besides GM and Honda, Daimler also has a large fleet of hydrogen 
                fuel cell cars and probably can still claim the title of having 
                the most FCVs (fuel cell vehicles) on the road. Most of the Daimler 
                hydrogen fuel cell cars are being used in Germany and other European 
                nations, except for a few that turn up occasionally at auto shows 
                in the U. S.</p>
              <p>Hydrogen fuel cell cars are considered to be zero emission vehicles 
                (ZEV) because they have no tailpipe emissions. Some will argue 
                that since most <a href="hydrogen-fuel.htm">hydrogen fuel</a> 
                is produced from natural gas that there are indeed &quot;well-to-wheel&quot; 
                emissions. </p>
              <p>But, when hydrogen fuel is produced using renewable energy such 
                as wind, solar, geothermal, or hydro-electric, it has zero emissions 
                well-to-wheel. When hydrogen is produced for fuel cells using 
                renewable energy it is done so by passing an electrical current 
                through water (H2O) and splitting it into H2 and O. </p>
              <p>The hydrogen is then compressed to 5,000 psi or 10,000 psi and 
                eventually pumped into a fuel cell car's specially constructed 
                tank. From there, the hydrogen from the tank plus oxygen in the 
                ambient air passes through the fuel cell. This creates an electrical 
                current that turns one or more electric motors that turn the wheels 
                of the car.</p>
              <h3><i>Battery Electric Vehicles?</i></h3>
              <p>In the past couple of years and especially with the Obama administration, 
                many people see battery electric vehicles instead of hydrogen 
                fuel cell cars as the near term solution to zero emissions, reducing 
                greenhouse gases that contribute to global warming and lessening 
                dependence upon foreign fossil fuels.</p>
              <p>Battery electric car advocates cite the lack of hydrogen refueling 
                infrastructure as the major hurdle in rolling out fuel cells cars 
                on a nationwide basis. And, they would be correct that the needed 
                infrastructure is not yet in place to support a rollout of millions 
                of hydrogen fuel cell cars.</p>
              <p>What would help with the infrastructure shortcoming however is 
                the advent of <a href="home-hydrogen-fueling-stations.htm">home 
                hydrogen fueling stations</a>. Companies such as Honda, ITM Power 
                and Hydrogenics have at least prototype home hydrogen pumps that 
                may be available soon. </p>
              <table width="664" cellspacing="2" cellpadding="2" height="128">
                <tr>
                  <td width="482">
                    <div align="center"><i><b>One of the problems with having 
                      hydrogen fuel cell cars for everyday use is the &quot;chicken 
                      or the egg&quot; syndrome.</b></i> </div>
                  </td>
                  <td width="158"><img src="images/hydrogenfuelcellcar.jpg" width="400" height="195" border="1"></td>
                </tr>
              </table>
              <p>Automakers don't want to build thousands or millions of hydrogen 
                fuel cell cars if the H2 fueling stations are not in place. The 
                manufacturers of fueling stations and those who run them do not 
                want to invest the money in them if there are only a handful of 
                hydrogen fuel cell cars on the road.</p>
              <p>It is because of this chicken-or-the-egg syndrome that the U. 
                S. is trying to use the &quot;hydrogen cluster&quot; model to 
                put fuel cell cars in the marketplace. This model uses high population 
                areas such as Los Angeles and New York City, and rolls out fuel 
                cell cars and hydrogen fueling stations concurrently.</p>
              <p>In Germany, which is far smaller than the United States, there 
                is an H2 Mobility plan in place. A consortium of companies and 
                government entities are racing to put up H2 fueling stations throughout 
                the country so that hydrogen fuel cell cars can be commercialized 
                in that nation by the year 2015. </p>
              <p>In summary, hydrogen fuel cell cars are still the future compared 
                to current battery electric vehicles. Hydrogen fuel cell cars 
                outperform battery electric vehicles on both range and refueling 
                time. Though a huge hydrogen refueling infrastructure will need 
                to be in place for fuel cell cars to take off, so will a huge 
                recharging infrastructure need to be in place to accommodate electric 
                cars.</p>
              <p>But, this isn't an either / or scenario. Both types of vehicles 
                plus biofuel vehicles will be part of the automotive future for 
                years to come. The goals are the same for all alternative fuel 
                vehicles including reducing emissions, reducing global warming 
                and reducing dependence upon foreign fossil fuels.</p>
