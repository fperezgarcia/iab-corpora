Here are five wonderful art and design projects, inspired by literary classics.

<b>1. <em>Wake in Progress</em></b></p><p>

In February of 2010, Paris-based designer and illustrator <a href="http://www.invisibledot.net/">Stephen Crowe</a> set out on an ambitious project -- to not only read James Joyce's <em><a href="http://www.invisibledot.net/">Finnegans Wake</a></em>, considered one of the most difficult works of fiction in the English language, but to also illustrate it. The result is <em><a href="http://wakeinprogress.blogspot.com/">Wake in Progress</a></em> -- a creative feat that's part <a href="http://www.brainpickings.org/index.php/2011/11/03/saul-bass-a-life-in-film-and-design/">Saul Bass</a>, part <a href="http://pinterest.com/brainpicker/edward-gorey/">Edward Gorey</a>, part <a href="http://www.brainpickings.org/index.php/2011/10/19/lynd-ward-box-set/">Lynd Ward</a>, and yet entirely its own and entirely terrific.<br/><br/><style type="text/css">

<p>
<b>2. <em>Every Page of Moby-Dick</em></b></p><p>

Since 2009, former high school English teacher and self-taught artist Matt Kish has been drawing <a href="http://everypageofmobydick.blogspot.com/">every page</a> of the 552-page Signet Classics paperback edition of Herman Melville's iconic <em><a href="http://www.amazon.com/dp/0451526996/ref=as_li_ss_til?tag=braipick-20&amp;camp=213381&amp;creative=390973&amp;linkCode=as4&amp;creativeASIN=0451526996&amp;adid=17SWD4BP4KZD6Z327GWM">Moby-Dick</a></em>, methodically producing one gorgeous, obsessive drawing per day for 552 days using pages from discarded books and a variety of drawing tools, from ballpoint pen to crayon to ink and watercolor. Last year, the project became <em><a href="http://www.amazon.com/dp/1935639129/ref=as_li_ss_til?tag=braipick-20&amp;camp=213381&amp;creative=390973&amp;linkCode=as4&amp;creativeASIN=1935639129&amp;adid=15QRGW97B5DXPM33C3CD">Moby-Dick in Pictures: One Drawing for Every Page</a></em> -- one of the <a href="http://www.brainpickings.org/index.php/2011/11/28/best-art-design-books-2011/">11 best art and design books of 2011</a>, gathering Kish's magnificent lo-fi drawings in a 600-page visual masterpiece of bold, breathtaking full-page illustrations that captivate eye, heart, and mind, inviting you to rediscover the Melville classic in entirely new ways.
</p><blockquote>
"I've read the book eight or nine times [...] Each and every reading has revealed more and more to me and hinted tantalizingly at even greater truths and revelations that I have yet to reach. Friends often question my obsession with the novel, especially since I am not a scholar or even an educator any longer, and the best explanation I have been able to come up with is that, to me, Moby-Dick is a book about everything. God. Love. Hate. Identity. Race. Sex. Humor. Obsession. History. Work. Capitalism [...] I see every aspect of life reflected in the bizarre mosaic of this book." ~ Matt Kish
</blockquote>

<br/><br/><p><b>3.<em> Word Bible Designs</em></b></p><p>

</p><p>In his <em><a href="http://jimlepage.com/word-designs/">Word</a></em> project, designer <a href="http://jimlepage.com/">Jim LePage</a> set out to create original designs for every book of the Bible, in an exercise in self-discipline that allowed him to marry his love of design with his desire to read the Bible more. Though the impetus for the project sets off my own <a href="http://www.brainpickings.org/index.php/2011/07/27/science-vs-religion-50-famous-academics-on-god/">religious alarms</a>, the Bible, too, is literature, and it's hard to dismiss the refreshing approach of this literary art project. Besides, perhaps this is the kind of secular silver lining Alain de Botton promised in <em><a href="http://www.brainpickings.org/index.php/2012/03/06/religion-for-atheists-alain-de-botton/">Religion for Atheists</a></em>.</p><br/><p>

<br/><br/><p><b>4. <em>Hark! A Vagrant</em></b></p><p>

From <em>New Yorker</em> cartoonist Kate Beaton comes <em><a href="http://www.amazon.com/dp/1770460608/ref=as_li_ss_til?tag=braipick-20&amp;camp=213381&amp;creative=390973&amp;linkCode=as4&amp;creativeASIN=1770460608&amp;adid=01DB4DPTTQT3WT4M3PNR">Hark! A Vagrant</a></em> -- a witty and wonderful collection of comics about historical and literary figures and events, based on her popular <a href="http://harkavagrant.com/">web comic</a> of the same name.</p><p>

Beaton, whose background is in history and anthropology, has a remarkable penchant for conveying the momentous through the inane, aided by a truly special gift for simple, subtle, incredibly expressive caricature. From dude spotting with the Bront Sisters to Jane Austen dodging groupies, the six-panel vignettes will make you laugh out loud and slip you a dose of education while you aren't paying attention.
</p><blockquote>
"I think comics about topics like history or literature can be amazing educational tools, even at their silliest. So if you learn or look up a thing or two after reading these comics, and you've enjoyed them, then I will be more than pleased! If you're just in it for the silly stuff, then there is plenty of that to go around, too." ~ Kate Beaton
</blockquote>

Beaton is also a masterful writer, her dialogue and captions adding depth to what's already an absolute delight.
</p><p>
<img alt="hark5.jpg" src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/static/mt/assets/culture_test/hark5.jpg" width="615" height="335" class="mt-image-center" style="text-align: center; display: block; margin: 0 auto 20px;width: 100.0% !important;max-width: 615px !important;height: auto !important;box-sizing:border-box;-webkit-box-sizing:border-box;-moz-box-sizing:border-box;"/><br/><br/><img alt="6.jpg" src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/static/mt/assets/culture_test/6.jpg" width="615" height="408" class="mt-image-center" style="text-align: center; display: block; margin: 0 auto 20px;width: 100.0% !important;max-width: 615px !important;height: auto !important;box-sizing:border-box;-webkit-box-sizing:border-box;-moz-box-sizing:border-box;"/><br/><br/><b>5. <em>Beholding Holden</em></b></p><p>

From writer Mike Norris and artist <a href="http://resemblancetheportraits.blogspot.com/">David Richardson</a> comes <em><a href="http://www.litkicks.com/BeholdingHolden">Beholding Holden</a></em>, an enchanted visual exploration of J.D. Salinger's <em><a href="http://www.amazon.com/dp/0316769177/ref=as_li_ss_til?tag=braipick-20&amp;camp=0&amp;creative=0&amp;linkCode=as4&amp;creativeASIN=0316769177&amp;adid=015JSFPAC5M3TYH320N5">The Catcher in the Rye</a></em>, a follow-up to their earlier collaboration on depicting the fictional <a href="http://www.litkicks.com/GlassFamilyAlbum">Glass family</a>.
<br/><br/></p><p class="caption">
<img alt="beholdingholden1.jpg" src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/static/mt/assets/culture_test/beholdingholden1.jpg" width="595" height="838" class="mt-image-center" style="text-align: center; display: block; margin: 0 auto 20px;width: 96.7% !important;max-width: 595px !important;height: auto !important;box-sizing:border-box;-webkit-box-sizing:border-box;-moz-box-sizing:border-box;"/>Holden Caulfield
</p>
<br/><br/><p class="caption">
<img alt="beholdingholden2.jpg" src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/static/mt/assets/culture_test/beholdingholden2.jpg" width="595" height="838" class="mt-image-center" style="text-align: center; display: block; margin: 0 auto 20px;width: 96.7% !important;max-width: 595px !important;height: auto !important;box-sizing:border-box;-webkit-box-sizing:border-box;-moz-box-sizing:border-box;"/>Old Mr. Spencer
</p>
<br/><br/><p class="caption">
<img alt="beholdingholden3.jpg" src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/static/mt/assets/culture_test/beholdingholden3.jpg" width="595" height="838" class="mt-image-center" style="text-align: center; display: block; margin: 0 auto 20px;width: 96.7% !important;max-width: 595px !important;height: auto !important;box-sizing:border-box;-webkit-box-sizing:border-box;-moz-box-sizing:border-box;"/>Ackley
</p>
<br/><br/><p class="caption">
<img alt="beholdingholden4.jpg" src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/static/mt/assets/culture_test/beholdingholden4.jpg" width="595" height="838" class="mt-image-center" style="text-align: center; display: block; margin: 0 auto 20px;width: 96.7% !important;max-width: 595px !important;height: auto !important;box-sizing:border-box;-webkit-box-sizing:border-box;-moz-box-sizing:border-box;"/>Stradlater, Holden's roommate at Pencey
</p>

</p>

<img alt="beholdingholden6.jpg" src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/static/mt/assets/culture_test/beholdingholden6.jpg" width="595" height="838" class="mt-image-center" style="text-align: center; display: block; margin: 0 auto 20px;width: 96.7% !important;max-width: 595px !important;height: auto !important;box-sizing:border-box;-webkit-box-sizing:border-box;-moz-box-sizing:border-box;"/>Sunny, the prostitute Maurice, an elevator operator moonlighting as pimp, offers Holden in Manhattan for 'five bucks a throw, fifteen bucks till noon.'

<p>
Norris and Richardson also collaborated on a fantastic <a href="http://www.litkicks.com/BeyondTheMadeleines">series of illustrations</a> based on Marcel Proust's <em><a href="http://www.amazon.com/dp/0812969642/ref=as_li_ss_til?tag=braipick-20&amp;camp=0&amp;creative=0&amp;linkCode=as4&amp;creativeASIN=0812969642&amp;adid=02PBW49F19QDCT0EXTME">In Search of Lost Time</a></em>, considered the <a href="http://www.brainpickings.org/index.php/2012/01/30/writers-top-ten-favorite-books/">third greatest book of the 20th century</a>.
</p><p>
